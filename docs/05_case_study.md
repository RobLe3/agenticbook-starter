# Doc 05: Author Case Study

> **Example world model doc for *Agentic Book Writing for Dummies* — Chapter 5.**
> The case study document answers: What gives the author the right to write this book?
> It provides the concrete evidence — specific projects, measurable outcomes, real experience —
> that your agent draws on for grounding examples throughout the manuscript.
> Without this document, your agent invents "the author has years of experience" — which is
> useless. With it, your agent writes "the author's first Milky Way photo, taken at Bortle 6
> from a suburban parking lot, used a 30-second exposure at ISO 3200 with a kit lens" —
> which is specific enough to teach.

## Author Background

> **Replace this section with your own background.** Be specific.
> The agent uses these details to write authentic "from the author's experience" moments.
> Generic credentials ("lifelong interest in photography") are useless to your agent.
> Specific evidence ("photographed the 2023 Perseid meteor shower from three locations,
> producing 12 usable frames out of 340") is what makes grounding possible.

The author of *First Light* discovered night sky photography during a camping trip in 2019. Unable to sleep, they pointed their phone at the Milky Way on a whim. The result was a muddy, blurry smear — but the stars were there. It took two more years, three cameras, and approximately 60 shooting nights before the author produced a photograph they were willing to share publicly.

**Relevant experience:**
- 60+ dedicated night sky photography sessions across 4 years (2019–2023)
- Equipment progression: iPhone 11 → Canon Rebel T7i → Sony A7III (current)
- Locations: suburban backyard (Bortle 7), rural state park (Bortle 4), dedicated dark-sky reserve (Bortle 2)
- Subjects photographed: Milky Way galactic core, star trails, meteor showers (Perseids, Leonids), lunar eclipses, conjunction events (Jupiter/Saturn 2020)
- Post-processing tools used: Lightroom Mobile, Adobe Lightroom Classic, RawTherapee, Sequator (for stacking)
- Published 3 images in regional astronomy club newsletter; 1 image selected for local nature photography exhibition

**What the author does NOT have (and the book doesn't claim):**
- Professional photography credentials
- Telescope or deep-sky experience
- Scientific astronomy training
- Photography degree or formal training

> **Why include what you don't have:** It sets the scope guardrails from Doc 01 in the agent's
> context. An agent that knows the author is NOT a professional astronomer won't write the book
> as if it is for professionals. Honesty about limits prevents overreach in generation.

> makes an abstract concept believable.
> If you cannot fill in a chapter's grounding evidence, that is a signal that you need
> more real experience before writing that chapter — or need to clarify what you actually know.

## Quotable Moments (Approved for Direct Use)

> These are specific "true stories" your agent can use in the manuscript as first-person or
> narrative examples. They are approved for use as written — the agent does not need to
> verify or pad them.
> Add 3–5 of your best "teaching moments" here.

**"The blurry comet"**
During the Leonid meteor shower of 2021, the author set up for a 2-hour session and discovered at 1am that the camera's autofocus had been on the entire time — every frame had autofocused on infinite darkness and returned to near focus, producing 200 frames of blurred stars. The lesson became Chapter 3's manual focus section.

**"The $0 tripod"**
The first star trail photo was taken in 2019 with a phone balanced on a backpack propped against a fence post. It worked. The photo is not great, but the stars are there. It became the proof-of-concept that the book doesn't require expensive gear — and Chapter 1's lab exercise uses this exact setup.

**"The wrong direction"**
In June 2021, the author drove an hour to a dark-sky location, set up perfectly — and then discovered they had set up facing north when the Milky Way core was due south. The entire core-visible window passed while the camera pointed at the Big Dipper. Chapter 4's planning section was built around preventing this specific failure.

## What This Book Does NOT Come From

> **Define the limits of your authority.** This prevents your agent from attributing expertise
> you don't have.

- Telescope astrophotography (deep-sky nebulae, galaxies) — the author has never owned a telescope
- Time-lapse or video — the author has not practiced this
- Competitive or professional photography — this is a hobby, not a profession
- Southern Hemisphere photography — all experience is from Northern Hemisphere locations
- Winter Milky Way photography — the author has only photographed the galactic core in spring/summer/early autumn
