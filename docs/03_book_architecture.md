# Doc 03: Book Architecture

> **Example world model doc for *Agentic Book Writing for Dummies* — Chapter 5.**
> This is your chapter outline. For Short flavor: 6–10 chapters is typical.
> Each chapter entry must specify INTENT (what the reader believes after reading)
> and OUTPUT (what the reader can produce after the lab).
> Your agent uses intent statements to stay on-topic during generation.

---

## Structure Overview

| Part | Chapters | Focus |
|------|----------|-------|
| Part I: Starting Now | Intro + Ch 1–2 | Smartphone photography, immediate first win |
| Part II: Going Further | Ch 3–5 | Entry DSLR, manual settings, location strategy |
| Part III: Going Deep | Ch 6–7 | Advanced technique, post-processing, planning |

**Total target:** 22,000–28,000 words
**Average chapter:** 2,800–3,500 words
**Introduction:** 600–800 words (no lab exercise)

---

## Introduction: Why the Night Sky

**Intent:** The reader understands that this book is designed for them — even if they've tried before and failed, even if they only have a smartphone. They believe the first win is within reach tonight.

**Word count target:** 600–800 words
**Lab exercise:** None
**Key content:**
- The photograph that inspired this book (author's first light experience — see Doc 05)
- What the book will and will not teach (scope from Doc 01)
- How to use the book (gear-progressive reading path)

> **Agent note:** Do not promise professional-quality output in the introduction. The promise is
> *a real photograph from your own equipment* — that is the emotional threshold, not "gallery quality."

---

## Chapter 1: Your First Night Out (Smartphone)

**Intent:** The reader believes that a meaningful night sky photograph is possible with what they already own, tonight, without any prior knowledge.

**Output of Lab 1:** One photograph of star trails or a bright planet, taken with a smartphone, that they can share.

**Word count target:** 2,500–3,000 words
**Sections:**
1. What your phone can actually see (dispelling the myth that phones can't do night photography)
2. The three settings that matter: night mode, long exposure, timer
3. Stability: the $0 solution (rocks, walls, improvised mounts) and the $15 solution (phone tripod)
4. Finding your first target: the Moon, Venus, or Jupiter on a clear night
5. **Lab 1:** Photograph a bright planet or the Moon with your smartphone

**Forward references:** Chapter 3 (when to upgrade to DSLR), Chapter 5 (dark-sky locations)

---

## Chapter 2: Reading the Sky

**Intent:** The reader can look at a weather forecast and a moon phase calendar and make a confident go/no-go decision for a shooting night.

**Output of Lab 2:** A completed planning checklist for their next shooting night (specific date, location, target object, conditions assessment).

**Word count target:** 2,500–3,000 words
**Sections:**
1. The three enemies: clouds, moon, and light pollution
2. Weather apps that matter for night photography (Clear Outside, Weather Underground)
3. Moon phase: why less is more, and how to find new moon dates
4. Light pollution maps: how to read a Bortle scale map, what each level means
5. **Lab 2:** Plan your next shooting night using the planning checklist

**Forward references:** Chapter 5 (choosing a dark-sky location)

> **Agent note:** The Bortle scale (1–9, where 1 is darkest) is defined in Doc 04.
> Do not introduce it here without referencing the glossary definition.

---

## Chapter 3: Moving to Manual (Entry DSLR)

**Intent:** The reader understands why manual mode exists and is not afraid of it. They know the three settings (ISO, aperture, shutter speed) and can set them correctly for a clear night with a kit lens.

**Output of Lab 3:** One in-focus photograph of stars (not trails) with a DSLR in manual mode.

**Word count target:** 3,000–3,500 words
**Sections:**
1. Why auto mode fails at night (and why that's not the camera's fault)
2. The exposure triangle: ISO, aperture, shutter speed, explained without math
3. The 500 rule for sharp stars (introduced and explained — see Doc 04)
4. Your first manual settings: ISO 3200, f/2.8 (or widest aperture), 20 seconds
5. Focus: why autofocus fails at night, and how to achieve manual infinity focus
6. **Lab 3:** Photograph a star field in manual mode; confirm sharpness at 100% crop

**This is the pivot chapter.** Readers who are smartphone-only can read this chapter to understand what awaits when they upgrade. Readers who already own a DSLR may start here.

---

## Chapter 4: Finding the Milky Way

**Intent:** The reader knows when and where the Milky Way is visible from their location, and can plan a shoot to capture the galactic core.

**Output of Lab 4:** A planning file for a Milky Way shoot: date, time, direction, location coordinates, and a phone-preview composition sketch.

**Word count target:** 2,800–3,200 words
**Sections:**
1. What the Milky Way actually is (and why we can only see part of it)
2. Galactic core season: when to see it, from which hemisphere
3. Planning apps: PhotoPills and Stellarium (free tier sufficient)
4. Composition basics: the foreground rule — why great Milky Way photos have something on Earth in them
5. **Lab 4:** Use PhotoPills or Stellarium to plan a specific Milky Way shoot

---

## Chapter 5: Finding Dark Skies

**Intent:** The reader can identify dark-sky locations within driving distance and evaluate them for access, safety, and shooting conditions.

**Output of Lab 5:** A shortlist of 2–3 accessible dark-sky locations with access notes, nearest services, and a conditions assessment.

**Word count target:** 2,500–3,000 words
**Sections:**
1. The Bortle scale revisited: what each level means for your photos
2. Light Pollution Map: finding Bortle 3–5 sites near you
3. Dark Sky Finder and Dark Sky Places directory (official certified sites)
4. Practical site evaluation: parking, horizon clearance, safety, cell coverage
5. **Lab 5:** Find and evaluate 2–3 dark-sky sites using the site evaluation checklist

---

## Chapter 6: Getting the Shot

**Intent:** The reader has a complete pre-shoot workflow (plan → check conditions → arrive → set up → shoot → back up) and can execute it confidently.

**Output of Lab 6:** A real Milky Way photograph from a dark-sky location.

**Word count target:** 3,000–3,500 words
**Sections:**
1. Pre-shoot prep: charged batteries, formatted cards, plan confirmed
2. On location: dark adaptation (20–30 minutes), red light use, site survey
3. Composition in the dark: using live view, framing by phone preview
4. Shooting sequence: test shot → adjust → sequence of 10 images → backup
5. What to do when it doesn't go as planned (clouds, dew, dead battery)
6. **Lab 6:** Execute a full Milky Way shoot and return with at least one usable frame

> **This is the "payoff" chapter.** The prose can be slightly more expansive here.
> This is the moment the reader has been working toward since Chapter 1.

---

## Chapter 7: From Raw to Print

**Intent:** The reader can process a raw Milky Way image to publication quality using free or low-cost software, and can export it for print or digital sharing.

**Output of Lab 7:** One fully processed, export-ready Milky Way photograph.

**Word count target:** 3,000–3,500 words
**Sections:**
1. Raw vs. JPEG: why raw matters for night photography
2. Lightroom Mobile (free) or RawTherapee (free, desktop): the minimal workflow
3. The five adjustments that matter: white balance, exposure, highlights, shadows, noise reduction
4. Saving for print vs. web: resolution, file format, color space
5. **Lab 7:** Process one image from Lab 6 to final export quality

**Closing section:** "What comes next" — pointing beyond Short-flavor scope to telescope astrophotography, image stacking, and advanced processing (without teaching these topics)

---

## Lab Exercise Design Notes

> **For Short flavor:** Labs should be completable in a single session (2–4 hours max).
> Every lab produces a tangible artifact — a file, a photo, a checklist, a plan.
> Labs are cumulative: Lab 5 builds on the plan from Lab 4; Lab 6 executes it.

| Lab | Output | Equipment needed |
|-----|--------|-----------------|
| Lab 1 | Star trails / planet photo | Smartphone + stable surface |
| Lab 2 | Shooting night plan | Any device (weather/moon apps) |
| Lab 3 | In-focus star field | DSLR or mirrorless + tripod |
| Lab 4 | Milky Way shoot plan | Any device (PhotoPills or Stellarium) |
| Lab 5 | Dark-sky site shortlist | Any device (map apps) |
| Lab 6 | Milky Way photograph | DSLR + tripod + dark-sky location |
| Lab 7 | Processed export-ready photo | Computer with Lightroom Mobile or RawTherapee |
