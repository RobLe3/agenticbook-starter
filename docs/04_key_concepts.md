## Photography Terms

**Aperture**
The opening in the lens that controls how much light reaches the sensor. Measured in f-stops (f/1.8, f/2.8, f/5.6). Lower f-number = wider aperture = more light. For night photography, always use the widest aperture your lens allows.
*First use: Chapter 3*

**Exposure triangle**
The relationship between ISO, aperture, and shutter speed. Changing one requires adjusting another to maintain the same overall exposure. In night photography, all three are pushed to their limits simultaneously.
*First use: Chapter 3*

**ISO**
The camera sensor's sensitivity to light. Higher ISO = more sensitive = brighter image = more digital noise. Night photography typically requires ISO 1600–6400 depending on the lens and conditions.
*First use: Chapter 3*

**Long exposure**
A photograph taken with a shutter speed longer than approximately 1 second. At 30+ seconds, star trails become visible. At 10–25 seconds, individual stars appear as points (not trails) if the 500 rule is followed.
*First use: Chapter 1*

**Manual mode**
A camera mode in which the photographer sets ISO, aperture, and shutter speed independently. Required for night sky photography — automatic modes cannot correctly expose for dark skies.
*First use: Chapter 3*

**Raw file (RAW)**
An unprocessed image file that contains all data the camera sensor captured. Larger than JPEG; requires software to process. Preferred for night photography because it preserves detail in shadows and highlights that JPEG discards.
*First use: Chapter 7*

**Shutter speed**
The duration the camera sensor is exposed to light. Measured in fractions of a second (1/250s) or whole seconds (30s). For stars that appear as points, shutter speed must be short enough to avoid visible star trailing — see 500 rule.
*First use: Chapter 3*

**500 rule**
A quick guideline for the maximum shutter speed that keeps stars appearing as points rather than trails: divide 500 by the focal length of your lens (in mm on a full-frame camera). Example: a 24mm lens → 500 ÷ 24 = ~21 seconds maximum. Crop-sensor cameras require adjustment (divide 500 by focal length × crop factor, typically 1.5 or 1.6).
*First use: Chapter 3*

## Astronomy Terms

**Bortle scale**
A 9-point scale measuring sky brightness from light pollution. Bortle 1 is the darkest possible sky (remote wilderness). Bortle 9 is a city center. Most suburban locations are Bortle 7–8. For Milky Way photography, Bortle 4 or below is recommended; Bortle 5–6 is workable.
*First use: Chapter 2*

**Dark adaptation**
The process by which the human eye adjusts to low light conditions. Full dark adaptation takes 20–30 minutes. Red light (from a headlamp with a red mode) does not disrupt dark adaptation; white light does. Checking your phone screen resets the clock.
*First use: Chapter 6*

**Galactic core**
The densest central region of the Milky Way galaxy, visible as the brightest, most colorful part of the Milky Way band. The galactic core is visible from the Northern Hemisphere roughly from March through October (best: May–August). From the Southern Hemisphere, it is visible year-round at greater elevation.
*First use: Chapter 4*

**Light pollution**
Artificial sky brightness caused by city and suburban lighting. Reduces the contrast between stars and the background sky. The primary obstacle to night sky photography for most readers.
*First use: Chapter 2*

**Milky Way**
The galaxy containing our solar system, visible from Earth as a pale band of light crossing the sky. What we see is the edge-on view of the galactic disk. The galactic core — the brightest, most photographically interesting section — is visible from Earth only during certain months and at certain times of night.
*First use: Introduction*

**Moon phase**
The portion of the Moon's surface visible from Earth, determined by its position relative to the Sun. New moon (invisible) is the best phase for night photography. A full moon is bright enough to overwhelm faint stars. Photography is workable within approximately 3 days of new moon.
*First use: Chapter 2*

## Equipment Terms

**DSLR**
Digital Single-Lens Reflex camera. A camera with a physical mirror system and interchangeable lenses. The reference camera type for Chapters 3–7. Distinguished from mirrorless cameras (no mirror) and smartphones (fixed lens).
*First use: Introduction*

**Kit lens**
The standard zoom lens bundled with most entry-level DSLRs. Typically 18–55mm, f/3.5–5.6. Adequate for starting out; the variable maximum aperture (f/3.5 at 18mm, f/5.6 at 55mm) means always use the wide end (18mm) for night photography to maximize aperture.
*First use: Chapter 3*

**Mirrorless**
A camera type that uses an electronic viewfinder instead of a physical mirror. Functionally identical to a DSLR for the purposes of this book. All DSLR instructions apply equally to mirrorless cameras.
*First use: Chapter 3*

**Tripod**
A three-legged camera support. Required for any exposure longer than approximately 1 second. For night photography, a tripod is non-negotiable from Chapter 1 onward (even for smartphones).
*First use: Chapter 1*

## Planning & Apps

**PhotoPills**
A paid ($10) mobile app for planning astrophotography shoots. Shows the position of the Milky Way, Moon, and Sun for any location and time. Used for planning lab exercises in Chapter 4.
*First use: Chapter 4*

**Stellarium**
A free open-source planetarium app (mobile and desktop). Shows current and future sky conditions including constellation positions, planet locations, and Milky Way visibility. Free alternative to PhotoPills for basic planning.
*First use: Chapter 4*
