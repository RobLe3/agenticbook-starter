# Character Psychology Guide — [NOVEL_TITLE]

**Project:** [NOVEL_TITLE]
**Flavor:** Novel ([WORD_COUNT_TARGET] words)

> **Note for agent:** This document exists because AI-generated fiction has a specific
> failure mode that prose quality cannot fix: characters whose behaviour is driven by
> plot requirements rather than psychological logic. A character whose decisions are
> consistent with their wound/desire/fear structure will feel three-dimensional even when
> the plot is simple. A character whose decisions serve only the plot will feel flat even
> when the prose is strong.
>
> The wound/desire/fear structure (also called the "ghost/wound/want" model in some
> screenwriting frameworks) works as follows:
> - **Wound:** A formative experience that shaped the character's fundamental belief about
>   how the world operates. Often in the past; never fully resolved.
> - **Desire:** What the character consciously wants — their stated goal. This is what they
>   tell themselves they are pursuing.
> - **Fear:** What the character is structurally avoiding — the cost of the desire, or the
>   thing they believe they cannot survive if it happens.
> - **Ghost:** The specific past event that haunts present choices — not a memory that
>   appears in flashback, but a shaping event whose effects are visible in the character's
>   decisions throughout the novel.
>
> When generating any scene, identify which character is the POV character, check their
> wound/desire/fear structure, and ask: what would this character do in this situation given
> what they are avoiding? The answer may not be the most plot-convenient response. That
> tension is the scene.

## Relationship Dynamics Table

> **Note for agent:** The "shift point" in each relationship is the moment in the novel
> when the dynamic fundamentally changes. Write toward the shift point, not past it.
> After the shift point, the relationship is different — not necessarily worse, but altered
> in a way that cannot be undone. The shift point is often the emotional climax of a
> chapter even when it is not its plot climax.
