## Logline

[LOGLINE]

> **Note for agent:** The logline is written in present tense regardless of the novel's past-tense
> narration. Present tense in a logline signals "this is happening to someone right now" — it creates
> urgency for the person reading about the project, not the person in the story. A logline in past
> tense reads like a summary of something already finished. The logline also names both protagonists
> implicitly (the archaeologist and the steward) without naming either — this signals the dual-timeline
> structure without requiring explanation.

- **[COMPARABLE_TITLE_1] ([COMPARABLE_YEAR_1])** — [COMPARABLE_DESCRIPTION_1]. *[NOVEL_TITLE]* fills the gap *[COMPARABLE_SHORT_TITLE_1]* leaves for readers who [COMPARABLE_GAP_1].

- **[COMPARABLE_TITLE_2] ([COMPARABLE_YEAR_2])** — [COMPARABLE_DESCRIPTION_2]. *[NOVEL_TITLE]* is structurally similar but fills the gap [COMPARABLE_SHORT_TITLE_2] leaves for readers who [COMPARABLE_GAP_2].

- **[COMPARABLE_TITLE_3] ([COMPARABLE_YEAR_3])** — [COMPARABLE_DESCRIPTION_3]. *[NOVEL_TITLE]* fills the gap *[COMPARABLE_SHORT_TITLE_3]* leaves for readers who [COMPARABLE_GAP_3].

> **Note for agent:** Comparable titles are framed as "fills the gap that X leaves" rather than
> "better than X" or "similar to X." "Fills the gap" is more useful because it defines the
> specific reader need this novel meets — it implies that readers of X exist, that they found X
> valuable, and that this novel offers something X did not provide. This framing is also more
> honest: it acknowledges the quality of the comparable work without claiming equivalence.
> Never write "fans of X will love Y" — this is marketing language that does nothing for the
> agent generating prose. Use the comparable titles to calibrate tone and ambition level.

> **Note for agent:** Target reader descriptions are psychological and behavioural, not demographic.
> "Adults 35–55 with university education" tells the agent nothing useful. "They finish the book and
> immediately look up the [HISTORICAL_GROUP_NAME] Confederation" tells the agent exactly what level of confidence to
> write historical detail at — confident enough that the reader wants to verify, not hedged so heavily
> that no detail lands. The secondary reader description ("they will notice if it isn't") tells the
> agent that domain accuracy in this novel is not optional. It is a contractual obligation to a
> portion of the readership.

## Scope Guardrails

> **Note for agent:** These guardrails exist because a dual-timeline novel about colonial history
> and marine archaeology presents enormous scope expansion risk. Every one of these guardrails
> reflects a direction the story could plausibly expand — and must not. When generating a scene
> that starts moving toward any of these, stop and redirect. The guardrails are not limitations
> on ambition; they are the discipline that makes the novel's actual ambition achievable. The most
> common AI failure in scope-rich fiction is defaulting to the genre conventions of adjacent genres:
> thriller (physical threat, chase, explosive climax), romance (romantic subplot with [SUPPORTING_CHARACTER_NAME]),
> historical panorama (comprehensive reconstruction of 1872 West Africa). These guardrails name
> those failure modes explicitly so the agent can recognise them when they appear.
