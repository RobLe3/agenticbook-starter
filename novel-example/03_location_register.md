# Location Register — The Glass Meridian

**Project:** The Glass Meridian
**Flavor:** Novel (82,000–88,000 words)

> **Note for agent:** Sensory anchors are the single most important element in this register.
> A sensory anchor is one specific, non-generic detail that makes a location unmistakably itself.
> When you are about to write a scene in any of these locations, read the sensory anchor first —
> not the physical description. The physical description gives you walls and distances. The
> sensory anchor gives you the thing that makes the reader feel they have arrived.
>
> Generic location writing occurs when an agent uses the physical description to generate
> atmosphere ("the dark archive smelled of old paper") without the specific detail that
> makes this archive, this harbour, this ship different from every other archive, harbour,
> and ship in fiction. The sensory anchor prevents this. Use it every time.

## Location 1: The Wreck Site — *Meridian Star*

**Physical description:**
The *Meridian Star* lies at 44 metres depth, 3.2 kilometres off Pico Passage in the Azores.
Port-side partial collapse — the hull has folded at roughly 35 degrees from the seabed,
leaving the starboard side relatively intact. The ship is approximately 58 metres long, built
in the clinker-on-frame style of Liverpool merchant vessels of the 1860s. The cargo hold is
intact with silt accumulation across the floor. The captain's cabin is in the partially
accessible stern section; the door is wedged open by collapsed rigging.

**Sensory anchor:**
The near-constant temperature at this depth in the Azores thermocline is approximately 11°C.
Mara notices this not as a temperature reading but as a physical fact: the water does not
change. Every other dive site she has worked has temperature gradients, seasonal variation,
the sensory information of a living ocean. Here, at this depth, the cold is static. It is
the temperature of preservation. It is the temperature of the inside of a copper cylinder
for a hundred and fifty years.

**Narrative function:**
This is where the investigation begins and where it must return. Every desk discovery Mara
makes — in Lisbon, London, Accra — will eventually route back to this physical object at the
bottom of the sea. The wreck site is not the mystery's source; it is its anchor. The copper
cylinder is found here in Chapter 2. The final dive, in Chapter 26, brings Mara back to
photograph the captain's cabin as documented evidence for the formal submission.

**Time-of-day note:**
Surface conditions affect dive scheduling but the wreck itself is below photosynthetic depth —
no light variation. Mara always descends into the same colourless cold. Dawn dives are
preferred because Pico Passage traffic is lowest; the ROV footage in Chapter 2 was shot at 06:40.

**Access / plot-relevant features:**
Manifest found in a copper-sealed document cylinder in the captain's cabin, lodged behind a
collapsed chart table. The copper seal (a soldered cap, not a wax seal) is the preservation
mechanism. Port-side collapse means approach must be from starboard — Tomás's route requirement.

## Location 2: Horta Harbour, Faial Island

**Physical description:**
Horta is the main port of the Azores, on Faial Island's south coast. The harbour wall is
famous among trans-Atlantic sailors for its painted murals — boats that have stopped here
since the 1940s have left their mark, a dense palimpsest of names, flags, and amateur art
running the full length of the breakwater. The Research Vessel *Atlântida* is moored at the
commercial dock, two berths east of the ferry terminal. Café Sport sits fifty metres from
the harbour gate — wooden interior, a bar covered with yacht burgees, a clientele that
mixes researchers, sailors, and fishing families without particularly caring about the distinction.

**Sensory anchor:**
The breakwater murals. You cannot walk the harbour perimeter without reading fragments of
other people's voyages — a boat name in Dutch from 1987, a compass rose in chipped blue paint,
a child's drawing of a whale that has been weather-worn into something abstract. Mara walks
this wall when she needs to think. It is the novel's primary image for accumulated record —
layers on layers, some faded, some still vivid, none complete.

**Narrative function:**
The operational base for the investigation's first act. The research team debrief happens at
Café Sport. Tomás receives intelligence about Voss's permit inquiries here. The harbour is
also where Mara makes her first call to Kwame — sitting on the breakwater wall at dusk,
looking at a Portuguese name painted over a faded English one.

**Time-of-day note:**
Morning: working harbour, fishing boats, ferry activity. The research team is usually back
by 14:00. Evening: Café Sport is the informal meeting point. The painted wall is best at
dusk when the low light catches the relief of layered paint.

**Access / plot-relevant features:**
*Atlântida* has a wet lab and a portable XRF analyser for preliminary field analysis.
Café Sport is where Tomás tells Mara about the Pico harbormaster's archive (Chapter 14).

## Location 3: Marine Research Centre, Faial

**Physical description:**
A converted warehouse near the commercial dock, shared between the *Atlântida* team and a
University of Lisbon oceanography project. Concrete floor, industrial lighting, stainless
steel work surfaces. Two wet storage tanks for artefact conservation. A portable XRF analyser
(Olympus Vanta series) is bolted to a wheeled cart. The document cylinder is brought here
after recovery, placed in an anaerobic storage bag within an hour of surfacing. The cylinder
is not opened here. Opening it requires controlled conditions that are not available in the
field lab.

**Sensory anchor:**
The anaerobic storage bag. It is translucent and gives the copper cylinder a blurred, underwater
appearance — as if the object is still submerged, still in the place Mara found it. She
photographs it in the bag before transport. The photograph appears twice in the novel: once when
she takes it (Chapter 3) and once when she shows it to Alderton in London (Chapter 8).

**Narrative function:**
First documentation site. The XRF analysis that confirms the copper composition and the
document cylinder's approximate manufacture date is done here. This is also where Mara first
suspects the cylinder was sealed deliberately — the solder is interior-applied, not exterior,
which suggests it was sealed from inside the ship rather than at a manufacturer.

**Time-of-day note:**
No meaningful variation. The lab is used mid-morning after dive returns, and again in the
evening when the team processes the day's data.

**Access / plot-relevant features:**
University of Lisbon team has their own section of the lab and is not part of Mara's project —
their presence requires Mara to be careful about what she discusses in shared spaces.

## Location 4: Torre do Tombo, Lisbon

**Physical description:**
The Portuguese National Archives occupy a building in the University of Lisbon campus designed
in the 1990s — functional, modernist, with the archival stacks below ground for climate control.
The reading room has long blond-wood tables, adjustable task lighting, and a silence so
practiced it has become the building's primary atmosphere. The restricted maritime series
(Section C, maritime records pre-1900) is in a sub-basement accessible through a door that
requires a staff access card. The door has a standard keypad. The logs show who enters.

**Sensory anchor:**
The smell of the reading room is not musty — this is a well-managed modern archive. It smells
of nothing in particular. The particular absence of smell is what registers. Every other archive
Mara has worked in has had a signature: damp stone, old binding glue, floor wax. The Torre do
Tombo smells of controlled air. It is the smell of institutional management. It is the smell
of Voss.

**Narrative function:**
The primary institutional obstacle. Three of Mara's visits here form the escalating confrontation
structure of Act Two. First visit: application filed, denied. Second visit: partial access
granted, Section C withheld. Third visit: Section C accessed through Isabel's facilitation.
The Torre do Tombo is where Voss's authority is concentrated, which means it is where Mara
must eventually succeed.

**Time-of-day note:**
Opening hours 9:30–17:30, Monday–Friday. The reading room thins out after 16:00. Isabel's
facilitation depends on the post-17:00 monitoring gap in Room 4.

**Access / plot-relevant features:**
Section C inventory is in the secondary catalogue (publicly accessible). The actual documents
require staff access card. Photocopier in Room 4 is not monitored after 17:30.

## Location 5: Imperial College London, South Kensington

**Physical description:**
Mara's department occupies the third floor of a building that faces the Exhibition Road side
of the campus — a view of the Victoria and Albert Museum's rear entrance, and, on clear days,
the Natural History Museum's terracotta towers. Her lab is shared with two postdoctoral
researchers who are politely present and professionally discreet. The Garvin Archive of
historical dive logs occupies six metal filing cabinets along one wall — a private collection
donated to the department in 2008 that has never been fully catalogued.

**Sensory anchor:**
The Garvin Archive cabinets are labelled in the donor's handwriting — small, precise capitals
in blue marker on cream card. The system is idiosyncratic: organised by dive year, then by
dive master, then by region. To find a specific log you have to know the system. Mara knows it.
She donated two years of her own fieldwork notes to it when she returned from David's funeral.
She has not opened those cabinets since.

**Narrative function:**
Chapter 8 London return. Mara comes home to collect the Garvin Archive logs for comparison
with Amara's log. The Garvin Archive becomes important later when it reveals that a 1963 dive
survey off Pico recorded anomalous debris consistent with the *Meridian Star* — a prior sighting
no one connected to the right ship.

**Time-of-day note:**
The lab is busiest 10:00–14:00. Mara works there late; she always has. The building empties
by 19:00. She prefers it empty.

**Access / plot-relevant features:**
The Garvin Archive is not publicly accessible — it is a departmental resource. Mara is the
informal custodian because no one else uses it. The 1963 dive log is in Cabinet 3, under
"Pico/Faial Survey — Carvalho, J."

## Location 6: The British Library, London

**Physical description:**
The St Pancras building. The main reading rooms are large, airy, pin-drop quiet. The
newspaper archive (where the 1872 *Liverpool Daily Post* shipping columns are held on
microfilm) is in a side reading room with lower ceilings and older equipment. The Calder
Estate papers are in the rare manuscripts section — a separate application process, a separate
reading room, and a prohibition on photography that requires a reader to take notes by hand.

**Sensory anchor:**
The microfilm reader. The *Liverpool Daily Post* shipping columns of 1872 are on a reel
that has been wound and rewound for fifty years; the film has the specific shimmer of
frequently-handled acetate. Mara runs the reel at a speed she misjudges the first time —
the text blurs, she overcorrects, the reel goes too far. The physical awkwardness of the
microfilm reader is a deliberate scene element: this is not digital research. It is slow
and manual and she has to come back three times.

**Narrative function:**
Chapter 9 London strand. Mara is cross-referencing the ship's name (*Meridian Star*) against
published shipping columns to establish that the voyage existed in any form of public record.
It did. The cargo description in the shipping column is "agricultural materials and navigational
instruments" — matching Calder's manifest exactly.

**Time-of-day note:**
Reading rooms open 9:30. Reader passes are required for the Calder Estate papers section.
Alderton's name is on the application as supporting sponsor — he has standing at the Library.

**Access / plot-relevant features:**
Calder Estate papers sealed until 2031 under a family deed of gift. One relevant box — Box 7,
"Correspondence and Shipping Records 1870–1874" — has a prior reading record showing it was
last accessed in 2003. The reader who accessed it in 2003 was affiliated with the University
of Oxford.

## Location 7: Cape Coast, Ghana (1872)

**Physical description:**
Cape Coast in 1872 is the administrative and commercial centre of the British Protectorate.
The Castle dominates the shoreline — British administration, dungeons below that every
resident knows the purpose of, the Union flag above. The company warehouses on the waterfront
handle palm oil, ivory, and manufactured goods. The Fante elder's compound where the
diplomatic commission was organised is three streets inland from the waterfront, behind a
wall of whitewashed laterite that marks it as a prosperous family property rather than a
public building.

**Sensory anchor:**
The morning light in the elder's compound. Amara has been to this compound three times
before embarkation — twice on family business, once to receive her engagement as steward.
The compound faces east, and in the early morning the light comes through a gap in the wall
at an angle that cuts the courtyard into two precise sections. The diplomatic commissioners
meet in the shaded half. Amara sits in the lit half and listens.

**Narrative function:**
Amara's first chapter (Timeline B, Chapter 3). Establishes who she is, what the mission is,
and what her role in it is. The compound scene is the last time Amara is in a place that
belongs to her people before boarding the ship.

**Time-of-day note:**
Early morning scenes in the compound; mid-morning embarkation at the waterfront. The novel
opens Amara's chapters on embarkation day — the compound flashback is a single paragraph
of memory, not an extended scene.

**Access / plot-relevant features:**
The diplomatic commission documents were originally held in this compound. The Fante elder's
family still lives here — Kwame discovers this in Chapter 18 when he visits Cape Coast from
Accra. One descendant knows the family history. She does not know about the copper cylinder.

## Location 8: The *Meridian Star* at Sea (1872)

**Physical description:**
A three-masted Liverpool merchant barque, approximately 55 metres in length, with a registered
capacity of 420 tons. The captain's cabin is aft, separated from the main deck by a short
companionway. The steward's quarters are on the lower deck, forward of the cargo hold —
a space with one porthole and two bunks, shared with the second steward. The quarterdeck at
the stern is Amara's writing location: after 22:00, in good weather, it is unoccupied except
for the watch. She writes standing at the taffrail.

**Sensory anchor:**
The taffrail at night. The ship's wake is phosphorescent in the warm Atlantic — a dim blue-green
trail disappearing into the darkness behind them. Amara writes by a hooded lamp she borrowed
from the ship's carpenter with an explanation that was technically true. The phosphorescence
is visible behind her if she turns. She does not turn. She writes facing the stern, her back
to the ship's direction of travel, her eyes on where they have been.

**Narrative function:**
The primary space of Timeline B. Six of Amara's fourteen chapters are set aboard ship. The
physical conditions of the ship — the cramped quarters, the watch schedules, the weather,
the cargo hold she is not supposed to enter — all generate the specific scenes that the log
records. The taffrail is the novel's only image of Amara that is genuinely private.

**Time-of-day note:**
Day scenes: steward's duties (serving meals, organising the commissioners' quarters, running
messages Calder never acknowledges receiving). Night scenes: writing at the taffrail, 22:00–midnight.

**Access / plot-relevant features:**
The copper cylinder is not described until Chapter 27 (Timeline B, final Amara chapter) — the
reader learns in the last Amara chapter that she placed it in the captain's cabin during the
final night before Pico Island. Calder did not see her do this.

## Location 9: Pico Island, Azores (1872)

**Physical description:**
Pico is dominated by Pico Mountain — a stratovolcano that rises 2,351 metres directly from the
Atlantic and is visible from Faial on clear days. The harbour at Madalena, on Pico's northwest
coast, is small by Atlantic standards. The harbormaster's office in 1872 is a stone building
adjacent to the customs quay, containing arrival and departure logs going back to the 1830s.
The *Meridian Star* makes an unscheduled port call here for "adverse weather" — Calder's log
entry — and stays for forty-eight hours.

**Sensory anchor:**
The weight of the mountain behind the harbour. You cannot be in Madalena and not be aware
of Pico Mountain — it fills the southeast quarter of the sky in a way that reads more as
geological presence than scenery. Amara describes it in one sentence in her log: *The mountain
is made of old fire. The harbour master did not ask who we were.* That sentence is the only
evidence of what happened during the forty-eight hour stay that is not in Calder's falsified
record.

**Narrative function:**
The pivot of both timelines. Something happened during the *Meridian Star*'s Pico port call
that Calder chose not to record — and that the Portuguese harbormaster recorded without
knowing its significance. Tomás locates the Pico harbormaster's archive in Chapter 14. The
archive is in the Madalena municipal building, still intact, not digitised.

**Time-of-day note:**
Amara's single Pico chapter is set at night — she writes by lamplight in the steward's
quarters while the ship is moored. Mara's visit to the Pico archive (Chapter 20) is daytime.

**Access / plot-relevant features:**
The Pico harbormaster's log for November 1872 is in the Madalena municipal archive. It has
not been transferred to a national archive. It is not in any database. Tomás knows the
current municipal archivist's father.

## Location 10: Accra, Ghana (2024)

**Physical description:**
Kwame's base. His apartment is in a compound in East Legon — a neighbourhood that mixes
established family houses with new construction, the older plots recognisable by their red
laterite walls, the newer ones by their perimeter security cameras. The National Archives of
Ghana are in a government complex near the State House — a 1960s building expanded in the
1980s with a reading room that has been waiting for digitisation funding since 2011. The Fante
community sources Kwame contacts are spread between Accra and Cape Coast.

**Sensory anchor:**
The reading room at the National Archives of Ghana. The fluorescent lighting has one fitting
that cycles very slowly — it dims and recovers over a period of about forty seconds, steady
enough that you stop noticing it consciously but irregular enough that it has a subliminal
rhythm. Kwame sits under the stable fitting and does not mention the cycling one to anyone.
The archivist has placed a handwritten sign under it: *Under Maintenance (Since Sept. 2023).*

**Narrative function:**
Kwame's discovery that the National Archives of Ghana holds a copy of the Fante diplomatic
commission's original documents — documents the British believed were the only copies, and
that they believed were destroyed in the Cape Coast Castle fire of 1886. The Ghanaian copies
were made by the commission's secretary, carried separately, and deposited with a Fante family
who donated their papers to the archive in 1978.

**Time-of-day note:**
Kwame visits the archive in the morning. His calls to Mara are in the early Accra evening,
which is late afternoon in the Azores — the calls happen across the time zone boundary,
which the novel renders as a slight temporal displacement in their exchanges.

**Access / plot-relevant features:**
The diplomatic commission documents are catalogued under "Fante Confederation Papers 1868–1873,
Family Accession 1978-04." The catalogue entry is in the main index. The documents have never
been cross-referenced against British maritime records because no researcher had a reason
to connect them to a ship manifest before Mara's discovery.
