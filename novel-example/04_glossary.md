# Domain Glossary — The Glass Meridian

> **Book Lab reference — Lab 3 (Chapter 6):** If you completed Lab 3, compare your domain glossary to this example — look for: 3-category structure (vs flat list), scene-specific activation notes, term count (30 across 3 categories vs Short's ~20).

**Project:** The Glass Meridian
**Flavor:** Novel (82,000–88,000 words)
**Terms:** 30 across 3 categories

> **Note for agent:** This glossary is structured in three categories because different terms
> activate in different scene types. Marine Archaeology terms appear in Mara's procedural
> scenes (dive preparation, lab work, documentation). Colonial West African History terms
> appear in Amara's chapters and in the research scenes where Mara and Kwame unpack
> what the diplomatic mission actually was. Narrative Craft Terms are the structural vocabulary
> you need to understand how this specific novel is built — dual POV, free indirect discourse,
> the interleaving logic. Before writing a dive scene, read Category 1. Before writing any
> Timeline B chapter, read Category 2. Read Category 3 before starting any chapter.
>
> A three-category structure prevents the most common failure mode in domain-specific
> fiction: the agent reaches for a technical term in the wrong scene type, over-explains it,
> or uses it decoratively rather than functionally. Each term here includes a Usage Note
> that tells you exactly how the term appears in *this* novel — not in textbooks.

---

## Category 1: Marine Archaeology (10 Terms)

---

### 1. Photogrammetry

**Definition:** A technique for creating accurate three-dimensional models of a site or object
by processing multiple overlapping photographs to calculate spatial coordinates.

**Usage note in this novel:** Mara uses "photogrammetry" in Chapter 2 as shorthand for
competence — she begins the photogrammetric survey of the wreck site before she has opened
the cylinder, because documentation precedes collection. When she explains it to Tomás,
she simplifies deliberately: *"A thousand photographs, and the computer makes them into one
model."* The word appears again in the submission documents in Chapter 26, in its technical
form.

---

### 2. ROV (Remotely Operated Vehicle)

**Definition:** An unmanned, tethered submersible used for visual survey and manipulation
tasks at depths impractical or unsafe for human divers.

**Usage note in this novel:** The ROV survey precedes the dive survey — standard protocol.
The ROV footage from Chapter 1 (Mara watches it in the ship's lab) is the first evidence
that the wreck is not on any registry. Mara references the ROV footage each time she needs
to establish timeline of discovery in official correspondence. The ROV is named *Petrel* by
the research team; this name appears once, in Chapter 1, and is not elaborated.

---

### 3. Anaerobic storage

**Definition:** Preservation technique in which an artefact is kept in an oxygen-free
environment to prevent oxidation and biological degradation.

**Usage note in this novel:** The document cylinder goes into an anaerobic bag within one
hour of surfacing — this is not explained to the reader as a procedure but shown as Mara's
immediate action. The anaerobic bag creates the blurred-underwater visual in the Marine
Research Centre (see Location 3). In the lab in Chapter 3, Mara explains only: *"It needs
to stay like this until we have controlled conditions."* The word "anaerobic" does not appear
in any dialogue — Mara thinks it; she does not say it.

---

### 4. XRF Analysis (X-ray Fluorescence)

**Definition:** Non-destructive analytical technique that identifies elemental composition
of a material by measuring fluorescent X-rays emitted when the material is stimulated by
a primary X-ray source.

**Usage note in this novel:** The portable XRF in the Faial lab confirms the copper composition
and approximate period of manufacture of the cylinder. The result ("copper content consistent
with Liverpool foundry production, 1850–1880") is the first verifiable link to a specific
manufacturing context. Mara reads the result on the Olympus Vanta's screen and calls Alderton
immediately. She does not explain XRF to him — he already knows.

---

### 5. Chain of custody

**Definition:** An unbroken, documented record of possession and handling of an artefact
from the moment of discovery to its current location, establishing legal and scholarly integrity.

**Usage note in this novel:** Chain of custody is Mara's professional reflex and her primary
strategic tool. Every document she produces, every photograph she takes, every transfer of
the cylinder is logged with timestamp, location, and witnesses. When Voss attempts to
question the legitimacy of Mara's access to the document, Mara's chain of custody log is
her response. The phrase appears in Chapters 3, 11, and 24.

---

### 6. Artefact conservation

**Definition:** The preventive and remedial treatment of archaeological objects to stabilise
their physical condition and slow or halt deterioration.

**Usage note in this novel:** The cylinder cannot be opened in the field — it requires
conservation lab conditions to unseal without damaging the document inside. This constraint
drives the Chapter 3–5 movement: Mara must transport the cylinder to London or Lisbon before
reading what is inside it. The conservation delay is a plot mechanism, but it is a realistic one.

---

### 7. In situ

**Definition:** In the original position or location; an artefact or feature examined or
documented at the site of discovery rather than after removal.

**Usage note in this novel:** Mara's choice to photograph the cylinder in situ before
removal (Chapter 2) is characterisation as much as method. She takes the in situ photographs
even though she is running low on dive time. The photographs become legally significant
when Voss's lawyers argue that the cylinder was removed without proper documentation.
They are wrong. Mara knew they would try.

---

### 8. Sediment stratigraphy

**Definition:** The study of layered sediment deposits to determine the sequence and timing
of site formation and burial events.

**Usage note in this novel:** The sediment stratigraphy around the wreck tells Mara the ship
sank quickly — not a gradual settlement but a rapid event, likely a structural failure. This
matters because a rapid sinking increases the probability that the cabin contents were
undisturbed. The stratigraphy argument appears in Mara's Chapter 4 site report, once, in a
single paragraph. It is not elaborated; it is used.

---

### 9. Provenance

**Definition:** The documented history of an object's origin and ownership, used to establish
authenticity and legal title.

**Usage note in this novel:** Provenance is the contested terrain of Act Three. Voss argues
that the cylinder's provenance is uncertain because its removal protocols were not performed
under Torre do Tombo oversight. Mara argues that marine artefacts from international waters
are not subject to Portuguese archive authority. The provenance dispute is deliberately
not resolved within the novel — it is referred to a joint academic commission in the epilogue.
That is historically accurate to how these disputes proceed.

---

### 10. Thermocline

**Definition:** A layer in a body of water in which temperature decreases rapidly with
increasing depth, creating a thermal boundary that affects light, sound, and biological
activity.

**Usage note in this novel:** The Azores thermocline at 40–50 metres depth maintains
near-constant temperatures of 10–12°C year-round. Mara references this in Chapter 2 when
explaining to the University of Lisbon team why the document might have survived: the cold,
stable conditions prevented the biological activity that would have degraded the copper seal
or penetrated the cylinder. The thermocline is not described as scenery; it is described
as chemistry.

---

## Category 2: Colonial West African History (10 Terms)

---

### 11. The Fante Confederation

**Definition:** A political alliance formed in 1868 among Fante chiefs in present-day
Ghana to collectively resist both British and Ashanti pressure through a united legislative
and military structure.

**Usage note in this novel:** The Fante Confederation is the historical entity whose
diplomatic activity the *Meridian Star* voyage was part of. It is not described as background
in any character's internal monologue — it is established in the novel's epigraph (a
brief documentary note before Chapter 1) and then assumed. Characters who know it (Amara,
Kwame) do not explain it. Characters who don't (Alderton, Voss) encounter it through the
documents, not through lectures.

---

### 12. Gold Coast Protectorate

**Definition:** The formal designation for the British-controlled coastal territories in
present-day Ghana from 1821 to 1901, when the Gold Coast Colony was established.

**Usage note in this novel:** Amara's Cape Coast is Gold Coast Protectorate territory.
This matters because the Fante Confederation's diplomatic mission was, in part, an attempt
to establish formal political standing that the Protectorate designation denied them.
The term appears in Amara's chapters without definition — she lives in it; she does not
label it.

---

### 13. Diplomatic commission (1872)

**Definition:** In the novel's specific usage: the formal group of Fante representatives,
including senior commissioners and supporting staff, who were travelling to London in 1872
to present a case for Fante political autonomy to the Colonial Office.

**Usage note in this novel:** This is the novel's central historical claim. The commission
is called "the commissioners" by Amara, "the diplomatic mission" in Mara's contemporary
chapters, and "the unauthorised delegation" in Calder's log. The three different names
for the same group are a deliberate marker of whose institutional frame is in use.

---

### 14. Waterhouse & Sons

**Definition:** In the novel: a Liverpool-based merchant and shipping company that operated
the *Meridian Star* and was contracted to transport the Fante commission without official
acknowledgment.

**Usage note in this novel:** Waterhouse & Sons is an invented company built on the model
of documented Liverpool merchant firms of the period. Their name appears in the manifest
header, in Calder's commission agreement (referenced in the Calder Estate papers), and in
the *Liverpool Daily Post* shipping column for the *Meridian Star*'s November 1872 voyage.
The company is never characterised beyond its institutional function — it is a contracting
entity, not a character.

---

### 15. The 1874 Anglo-Ashanti War

**Definition:** A military conflict between the British Empire and the Ashanti Confederation
in which the British defeated Ashanti forces and sacked Kumasi, effectively ending Ashanti
political independence in the region.

**Usage note in this novel:** The 1874 war is the historical context that gives the 1872
diplomatic mission its urgency. The commissioners knew that the political window was closing.
Amara knows this. The novel does not explain it — it appears in a single sentence in Amara's
opening chapter: *"The war that everyone knows is coming has not happened yet."* Readers
with historical knowledge will understand; readers without it will understand that time is short.

---

### 16. Oral vs. written record in colonial contexts

**Definition:** The divergence between what colonial administrations recorded in official
documents and what colonised communities preserved through oral tradition, family memory,
and community practice.

**Usage note in this novel:** This is a structural principle, not a term that appears in
the prose. The novel's entire architecture is built on the tension between official written
record (Calder's manifest, the Torre do Tombo catalogue) and records that survived outside
institutional custody (Amara's log, the Ghanaian community's diplomatic documents). Never
explain this as a theme. Dramatise it through specific documents and specific silences.

---

### 17. Cape Coast Castle

**Definition:** A UNESCO World Heritage Site on the Ghanaian coast; originally a Portuguese
trading fort, later a major British slave-trading post, and in 1872 the seat of British
administrative power in the Gold Coast Protectorate.

**Usage note in this novel:** The Castle is visible from the elder's compound in Amara's
first chapter. It is not described in detail — its presence is simply noted, and its function
understood. The Castle fire of 1886 (which destroyed many colonial administrative records)
is relevant to Kwame's research: the British assumed the fire destroyed the Fante diplomatic
commission's documentary record. It did not destroy what was held in private family custody.

---

### 18. Fante merchant and diplomatic networks

**Definition:** The commercial and political relationship systems through which Fante trading
families operated, often maintaining connections across Cape Coast, the Ashanti interior, the
Atlantic trade routes, and British administrative structures simultaneously.

**Usage note in this novel:** Amara's family background (Fante merchant class, literate in
English and Fante, with connections to both the mission school and the waterfront trade) is
possible only because of these networks. Her engagement as steward was arranged through a
network connection, not through colonial employment channels. The novel does not elaborate
this — it is embedded in how Amara speaks about the people she knows.

---

### 19. Mission school education (19th century Gold Coast)

**Definition:** Primary and secondary schooling provided by European Christian missions in
the Gold Coast from the 1820s onward, which produced a generation of literate Fante graduates
who occupied ambiguous positions between colonial and Fante authority structures.

**Usage note in this novel:** Amara's Cape Coast mission school education is the source of
her written English — and of her knowledge of how official documents work. She knows what
a manifest is, what it is supposed to contain, and what Calder's contains instead, because
she has learned to read institutional documents alongside the biblical texts she was nominally
being educated to interpret. This is established in one paragraph in Chapter 3. It is not
revisited.

---

### 20. The restricted colonial archive

**Definition:** In the novel's usage: archival collections containing colonial administrative,
commercial, or diplomatic records that have been formally sealed, restricted, or
inadequately catalogued — either through explicit policy or institutional neglect.

**Usage note in this novel:** Both the Torre do Tombo's Section C and the Calder Estate
papers at the British Library are restricted collections. The novel positions restriction
not as villainy but as the normal bureaucratic face of institutional self-protection. The
Ghanaian family papers at the National Archives of Ghana are, by contrast, accessible — they
just haven't been connected to the right question yet.

---

## Category 3: Narrative Craft Terms (10 Terms)

---

### 21. Dual timeline

**Definition:** A novel structure in which two chronologically separate story strands are
told in alternating chapters, with thematic or plot connections that accumulate across both.

**Usage note in this novel:** The Glass Meridian runs Timeline A (2024, Mara's investigation)
and Timeline B (1872, Amara's voyage) in interleaved chapters. The connection between
timelines is asymmetric: the reader learns what happened in 1872 before Mara does. This
creates a dramatic irony that sustains tension without thriller mechanics. When writing
chapter sequences, check the interleaving logic in Doc 05 before deciding which timeline
follows which.

---

### 22. Deep third person limited

**Definition:** A POV mode in which the narration is grammatically third person but
psychologically aligned so closely with one character that the narrator's voice and
the character's internal experience are nearly indistinguishable.

**Usage note in this novel:** Timeline A chapters use deep third limited aligned with Mara.
Timeline B chapters use deep third limited aligned with Amara. Neither character sees the
other's chapters. Never shift POV within a chapter. The reader's synthesis of both timelines
is the structural experience of the novel.

---

### 23. Free indirect discourse

**Definition:** A narrative technique in which a character's thoughts or speech are
incorporated into the narration without quotation marks or speech tags, blending the
character's register with the narrator's voice.

**Usage note in this novel:** Mara's observations about Voss use free indirect discourse
frequently — the narration shifts briefly into Mara's sceptical register, then returns to
neutral. Amara's log entries use it differently: because Amara is writing, her free indirect
discourse has an editorial quality — she is not only thinking something, she is choosing
whether to write it. The hesitation is sometimes visible.

---

### 24. Thematic rhyme

**Definition:** The structural technique in which two scenes or chapters from different
timelines or locations echo each other's emotional or thematic content, creating meaning
through juxtaposition rather than explicit connection.

**Usage note in this novel:** Each Timeline B chapter is placed to rhyme with the Timeline A
chapter it follows. The rhyme is not always obvious; sometimes it is submerged. Examples:
the Chapter 2 dive (Mara descends into cold stillness) is followed by the Chapter 3 Amara
embarkation (she descends into the ship's lower deck for the first time). Both scenes are
about entering a confined space that holds something important. Never make the rhyme explicit
in the prose.

---

### 25. Dramatic irony

**Definition:** A condition in which the reader knows something that one or more characters
do not, creating a gap between what the character understands and what the reader perceives.

**Usage note in this novel:** The novel's primary dramatic irony: the reader accompanies
Amara in sealing the copper cylinder (Chapter 27, Timeline B) before Mara discovers what
it contains in full (Chapter 24, Timeline A). The chapter ordering creates this inversion
deliberately. When generating Timeline B chapters, know that the reader is accumulating
knowledge that Mara does not yet have.

---

### 26. Midpoint reversal

**Definition:** A structural shift at approximately the halfway point of a novel in which
a significant revelation or change of circumstances reframes the reader's understanding of
the story's direction.

**Usage note in this novel:** The midpoint reversal is Alderton's reveal (Chapter 17):
Mara discovers that her most trusted intellectual ally knew about the restricted collection
footnote twenty years ago and said nothing. This does not break the investigation — it
continues — but it changes the emotional landscape. Mara is now working without her primary
support structure. The second half of the novel is lonelier than the first.

---

### 27. Ellipsis (narrative)

**Definition:** A gap in the narrative where time passes without scene-level representation;
the reader is expected to infer what happened in the interval.

**Usage note in this novel:** Amara's chapters are log entries, not continuous narration.
There are ellipses between her chapters — sometimes days pass, sometimes weeks. The ellipses
are not marked with white space or chapter notes. The reader infers the gap from the date
header at the start of each Amara chapter and from what has changed (the weather, the ship's
position, the commissioners' mood).

---

### 28. Unreliable documentation

**Definition:** A narrative device in which a document within the story (a letter, a log,
a manifest) is presented to the reader with clear signals that it is inaccurate, incomplete,
or deliberately falsified — creating tension between what the document says and what the
reader suspects.

**Usage note in this novel:** Calder's manifest is the primary unreliable document. The reader
encounters it through Amara's account of what she observed (which contradicts the manifest)
before Mara encounters the manifest itself. Voss's communications with Mara function as
secondary unreliable documentation — she does not lie, but her omissions are structural.
When writing Calder scenes, the unreliability should be legible to the reader without being
signalled by the narrator.

---

### 29. Structural silence

**Definition:** The deliberate absence of information at a narratively significant moment —
what the novel does not say, which becomes meaningful through context.

**Usage note in this novel:** The ending of Amara's story is a structural silence. The novel
never tells us what happened to Amara after Liverpool. The epilogue tells us what happened to
her log. The difference between those two facts is the novel's final emotional note. Do not
fill this silence. It is load-bearing.

---

### 30. Epistolary layer

**Definition:** Elements of a novel that are presented as reproduced documents — letters,
logs, reports, newspaper extracts — rather than narrated or dramatised scenes.

**Usage note in this novel:** Amara's log entries, reproduced in full in her chapters, are
the novel's epistolary layer. The *Liverpool Daily Post* shipping column excerpt (Chapter 9)
is a second, briefer epistolary element. The Torre do Tombo catalogue inventory (Chapter 12)
is a third. Each epistolary element is formatted distinctly in the manuscript (indented,
smaller type) to signal its documentary nature. When generating these sections, maintain
Amara's precise, compressed register — do not let the narration bleed into the log.
