# Character Psychology Guide — The Glass Meridian

**Project:** The Glass Meridian
**Flavor:** Novel (82,000–88,000 words)

> **Note for agent:** This document exists because AI-generated fiction has a specific
> failure mode that prose quality cannot fix: characters whose behaviour is driven by
> plot requirements rather than psychological logic. A character whose decisions are
> consistent with their wound/desire/fear structure will feel three-dimensional even when
> the plot is simple. A character whose decisions serve only the plot will feel flat even
> when the prose is strong.
>
> The wound/desire/fear structure (also called the "ghost/wound/want" model in some
> screenwriting frameworks) works as follows:
> - **Wound:** A formative experience that shaped the character's fundamental belief about
>   how the world operates. Often in the past; never fully resolved.
> - **Desire:** What the character consciously wants — their stated goal. This is what they
>   tell themselves they are pursuing.
> - **Fear:** What the character is structurally avoiding — the cost of the desire, or the
>   thing they believe they cannot survive if it happens.
> - **Ghost:** The specific past event that haunts present choices — not a memory that
>   appears in flashback, but a shaping event whose effects are visible in the character's
>   decisions throughout the novel.
>
> When generating any scene, identify which character is the POV character, check their
> wound/desire/fear structure, and ask: what would this character do in this situation given
> what they are avoiding? The answer may not be the most plot-convenient response. That
> tension is the scene.

## Primary Characters: Wound / Desire / Fear / Ghost

---

### Mara Osei

**Wound:** Mara's father, a civil servant, told her at 14 that her mother's family was "too
Ghanaian" for British professional life — that her dual identity would make her less serious
in both contexts. She has spent her career proving this wrong through meticulous, unimpeachable
professional competence. The competence is real. The motivation behind it is unresolved.

**Desire:** To recover the Fante diplomatic commission from institutional silence and publish
the evidence. This is her stated goal. Consciously, she frames it as a professional obligation.

**Fear:** That the recovery will not change anything — that the archive will acknowledge
the error, file the correction, and move on without consequence, leaving the commission
documented but not remembered. This fear is related to a deeper fear she cannot yet articulate:
that doing the methodical, correct thing is not enough. That it never was.

**Ghost:** David's death. Not the grief itself — she has functional access to grief. What haunts
her is the eighteen months she spent after his diagnosis being perfectly organised, perfectly
present, perfectly useful to him — and the private knowledge that she was also holding something
back. She does not yet know what it was. The investigation is, structurally, the same act:
being methodical and useful in the face of something that cannot be fixed by methodology.

**How this structure activates in scenes:** When Voss obstructs her, Mara does not rage — she
documents. This is both professional best practice and avoidance: if she can keep adding to
the evidence chain, she does not have to confront what happens when the evidence chain ends.
Her impatience appears as increased precision, not anger. Watch for scenes where Mara's
response to emotional pressure is to do more fieldwork.

---

### Amara Quaye

**Wound:** Amara was educated by a mission school to be useful to the colonial enterprise
in ways that would always be credited to someone else. She is more capable than any of her
employers. She is structurally unable to say so. The wound is not humiliation — it is the
chronic gap between what she knows and what she is permitted to appear to know.

**Desire:** To produce a record that will outlast the commission's failure. She does not
expect to be believed in 1872. She is writing for someone who does not exist yet.

**Fear:** That she misjudged the situation — that the cylinder will be found by the wrong
person, or that her log is not, after all, legible to anyone who is not already looking for it.

**Ghost:** A conversation in the elder's compound, the week before embarkation. The senior
commissioner told her that she was not officially part of the mission — she was simply staff.
She thanked him. She had already decided to keep the log. The gap between what she said and
what she had decided is the founding act of her character.

**How this structure activates in scenes:** Amara's precise compression — she records facts,
not feelings — is both her strength and her coping mechanism. When something moves her, the
log gets shorter. The reader can measure her emotional response by what she omits. In Chapter
22, when she writes "I am going to seal this," it is the most personal sentence in the log.

---

### Dr. Helena Voss

**Wound:** Voss came to her career as a German scholar in a Portuguese institution, acutely
aware that her authority depended on being more rigorous, more careful, and more procedurally
correct than her colleagues. She earned her position through genuine intellectual work and
through a permanent, anxious performance of institutional trustworthiness.

**Desire:** To maintain the scholarly consensus she has built — not because it is expedient,
but because she genuinely believes it was correct with the evidence available to her. She is
defending her work, not a lie.

**Fear:** That she will be exposed not as a fraud but as someone who stopped asking questions
at the moment when asking questions became professionally costly. This is the more devastating
charge: not dishonesty but professional cowardice.

**Ghost:** The footnote, in a journal article, 2003. She found a reference to the restricted
Section C collection in the Torre do Tombo secondary catalogue while preparing a paper on
Azorean maritime records. She reviewed the catalogue description. She decided the collection
was probably administrative, probably not relevant to her argument. She filed the footnote.
She did not open the collection. She has not been able to explain to herself, under direct
self-examination, whether she decided it was not relevant before or after she noticed that
it might be.

**How this structure activates in scenes:** Voss is never rude and never visibly anxious.
Her responses under pressure become more formal, more procedural, more precise. Watch for
scenes where her precision increases — this is the tell. In Chapter 24, when she makes Mara
an implicit offer, she frames it in process language. Mara hears the subtext. Voss is counting
on her not to.

---

### Prof. James Alderton

**Wound:** Alderton's scholarship has always been grounded in the idea that academic rigor
is a moral practice — that doing the work carefully is itself an ethical act. This belief
has made him a genuinely good mentor and a principled scholar. It has also allowed him to
conflate "doing the work I did carefully" with "having done the right thing."

**Desire:** To remain, in Mara's eyes and in his own, the person who helped her become who
she is. His mentorship of Mara is the professional relationship he is most proud of.

**Fear:** That Mara will see, clearly, that he protected his own published work by not
pursuing a lead that would have reframed it — and that she will conclude he is not the
person he has represented himself to be.

**Ghost:** His 2004 Oxford lecture: "The Limits of Evidence in Colonial Maritime History."
He gave this lecture the year after he found the footnote. He argued that historians should
resist overextending claims beyond the archival evidence available. He believed this when he
said it. He has never been sure, since 2004, whether he also said it because saying it made
the footnote he had not followed less of an omission.

**How this structure activates in scenes:** Alderton is warm in the scenes before Chapter 17
because he is not performing warmth — it is real. After Chapter 17, the warmth is still real,
but it now coexists with Mara's knowledge of what it costs him to maintain it. Generate his
post-revelation scenes with this double register. He does not become cold. He becomes slightly
more deliberate. He chooses his warmth now.

## Relationship Dynamics Table

> **Note for agent:** The "shift point" in each relationship is the moment in the novel
> when the dynamic fundamentally changes. Write toward the shift point, not past it.
> After the shift point, the relationship is different — not necessarily worse, but altered
> in a way that cannot be undone. The shift point is often the emotional climax of a
> chapter even when it is not its plot climax.

---

### Mara / Kwame

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Current dynamic | Protective sibling solidarity. Kwame manages Mara's re-entry into functional life with care and without making it visible as care. Mara accepts help from Kwame in ways she accepts it from no one else. |
| Power balance | Equal but different register. Mara has institutional authority (academic credibility, peer review, chain of custody). Kwame has informational networks (community sources, investigative journalism skills, access to Accra). Neither can do what the other does. |
| Shift point | Chapter 16: Kwame reads the Ghanaian archive catalogue entry to Mara on the phone. His voice changes slightly. She notices. He notices she notices. Neither of them says what it means that this is their family's history they are recovering together. The shift is in what goes unsaid. |
| Post-shift | Kwame publishes the story. The epilogue's final image — he sends Mara a printed copy with the note "Nana would have known this already" — is the resolution of what went unsaid in Chapter 16. |

### Mara / Alderton

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Current dynamic | Trusted intellectual alliance. Mara reads Alderton as the person who always tells her the truth about her work. She has built a significant portion of her professional identity on his regard. |
| Power balance | Structurally unequal (supervisor/former student) but practically equalised by Mara's subsequent professional achievement. Alderton understands he is no longer her supervisor; he is her most trusted colleague. |
| Shift point | Chapter 17: the footnote. The shift is not dramatic — Alderton tells her quietly, in his office, over a cup of tea he made himself. The quiet is the point. This is not a revelation delivered for effect. It is an older man finally saying something he should have said years ago, with full awareness that saying it now is still too late. |
| Post-shift | The relationship survives but changes. Mara uses Alderton's British Library access and name in the final arc because she needs it and because he offers it. She accepts his help. She no longer reads his counsel as objective. That is a permanent change. |

### Mara / Voss

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Current dynamic | Formal institutional opposition. Voss follows procedure; Mara works within it and around it. Neither is personally hostile. They are professionally incompatible. |
| Power balance | Structurally favours Voss (she controls the archive). Practically shifting toward Mara (the evidence chain is accumulating outside Voss's jurisdiction). |
| Shift point | Chapter 24: Voss's implicit offer. She tells Mara that a joint publication, acknowledging the "inadvertent archival gap," might serve everyone's interests. It is not a threat. It is an invitation to reframe what happened as an error rather than a suppression. Mara says she will consider it. They both know she won't. This is the moment the institutional contest is decided — without a scene of revelation or confrontation, by a single exchange in a coffee room. |
| Post-shift | Voss faces a formal inquiry. The novel does not show it. The reader is told this in a single sentence in Chapter 27. Voss's professional fate is not the novel's concern; the recovery of the record is. |

### Amara / Calder

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Current dynamic | Invisible asymmetry. Calder knows Amara exists. He does not consider what she knows or notices. She knows everything that he does not consider worth concealing from a steward. |
| Power balance | Absolute institutional power in Calder's favour. Calder commands the ship, controls the log, determines what is recorded. Amara has no official standing on the ship. She has the notebook. |
| Shift point | Chapter 25 (Timeline B): the sealing scene. Amara enters the captain's cabin at 03:00 and places the cylinder behind the chart table. Calder is asleep. He will never know she was there. The power balance inverts at the moment of a sleeping man, a copper cylinder, and a woman who has decided that documentation outlasts authority. |
| Post-shift | There is no post-shift within the novel. Calder never knows. The shift exists only in the reader's understanding. This is intentional. |

### Mara / Isabel

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Current dynamic | Cautious professional transaction. Isabel provides exactly what is needed with no elaboration. Mara uses exactly what she is given. Each respects the other's exposure. |
| Power balance | Procedurally, Voss's authority governs both of them within the archive. Practically, Isabel's knowledge of Section C, the monitoring gaps, and the catalogue structure gives her a specific operational leverage that neither Voss nor Mara possess. |
| Shift point | Chapter 21: Room 4, after 17:30. The moment Mara copies the Waterhouse & Sons instruction document, Isabel is logging records in the adjacent room. Neither of them will discuss this. The shift is that Mara now knows Isabel acted on principle, not procedure, and Isabel knows Mara knows. |
| Post-shift | One late scene (Chapter 27): they pass each other in the Torre do Tombo hallway. Isabel says "good afternoon." Mara says "good afternoon." The reader has the full context. The characters do not elaborate. That is the correct register for this relationship. |
