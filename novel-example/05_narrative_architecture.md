# Narrative Architecture — The Glass Meridian

> **Book Lab reference — Lab 5 (Chapter 8):** If you completed Lab 5, compare your narrative architecture to this example — look for: chapter intent as epistemic shift (not plot summary), dual-timeline chapter structure, Prologue/Epilogue handling.

**Project:** The Glass Meridian
**Flavor:** Novel (82,000–88,000 words)
**Structure:** Prologue + 28 chapters + Epilogue (30 total entries)

> **Note for agent:** "Chapter intent" is the most important field in each chapter entry.
> It answers the question: what must CHANGE in the reader's understanding by the end of this
> chapter? This is different from "what happens" (the plot summary) and different from
> "what the scene is about" (the thematic description). A chapter intent defines a specific
> epistemic shift: the reader knows something they did not know before, or understands
> something differently than they did before. When you are generating a chapter and you
> lose the thread, return to the chapter intent. Ask: have I delivered this shift yet?
> If not, the chapter is not finished, regardless of word count.

| Element | Value |

| Chapters 1–28 | Alternating A (2024) and B (1872); see interleaving logic below |
| Epilogue | Present tense, 18 months after the main action, both timelines implied |
| Total chapters | 30 narrative entries (Prologue + 28 + Epilogue) |
| Timeline A chapters | 14 (Mara, 2024) |
| Timeline B chapters | 14 (Amara, 1872) |
| Act structure | 5-act (see arc overview table) |
| Average Timeline A chapter | 3,000–3,200 words |
| Average Timeline B chapter | 2,500–2,800 words |

## Dual-Timeline Interleaving Logic

The two timelines do not simply alternate A/B/A/B. The sequencing logic is thematic:
each Timeline B chapter is selected to rhyme with the emotional or investigative content
of the Timeline A chapter it follows. The rhyme is not always obvious — sometimes it is
ironic, sometimes it is structural, sometimes it is a single repeated image seen from
both sides of 150 years.

**General pattern:** Timeline A opens, Timeline B responds.
The novel begins and ends with Timeline A. This is Mara's investigation;
Amara's chapters are the answer that arrives progressively.

**Irony principle:** The reader learns what happened in 1872 before Mara does.
By the time Mara pieces together the Pico Island port call (Chapter 20),
the reader has already accompanied Amara through it (Chapter 19).
This asymmetry is the engine of the novel's tension.

**Compression principle:** Amara's fourteen chapters span approximately 8 weeks
(Cape Coast embarkation to Liverpool arrival). Mara's fourteen chapters span
approximately 9 months (first dive to formal submission). The chronological
compression of Amara's story gives her chapters a different weight — each one
covers more historical time per word.

## Arc Overview: 5-Act Structure

| Act | Chapters | Description | Key Turn |
|-----|----------|-------------|----------|
| Act I: Setup | Prologue + Ch 1–4 | Mara arrives; cylinder recovered; first calls placed | Ch 4: solder analysis — deliberate sealing |
| Act II: Confrontation | Ch 5–12 | Institutional access fight; Amara establishes her record | Ch 12: Isabel's offer breaks the stalemate |
| Act III: Complication | Ch 13–17 | Evidence accumulates; Pico mystery deepens; Alderton's betrayal | Ch 17: midpoint reversal — Mara alone |
| Act IV: Crisis | Ch 18–24 | Pico confirmed; Ghanaian documents found; Voss's final obstruction | Ch 24: Voss's implicit threat — decision point |
| Act V: Resolution | Ch 25–28 + Epilogue | Cylinder's origin confirmed; submission; publication; naming | Ch 28 + Epilogue: Amara named publicly |
