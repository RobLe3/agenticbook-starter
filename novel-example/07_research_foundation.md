# Research Foundation — The Glass Meridian

**Project:** The Glass Meridian
**Flavor:** Novel (82,000–88,000 words)

> **Note for agent:** Fiction needs a source inventory for the same reason non-fiction does:
> a hallucinated historical claim breaks reader trust faster than any prose problem.
> In literary fiction with domain-specific content (marine archaeology, colonial history,
> period chemistry), a single wrong detail activates reader scepticism about everything
> around it. A reader who knows that the Fante Confederation's founding date is 1868 and
> finds it written as 1866 in Chapter 5 will spend the rest of the novel checking other facts
> rather than inhabiting the story. This document prevents that.
>
> The structure below separates claims into three tiers:
> 1. **Known-reliable:** Verifiable historical or scientific fact. Generate with confidence.
>    If the novel contradicts these, the novel is wrong and must be corrected.
> 2. **Plausible:** Claims consistent with the historical record but not individually
>    documented — plausible given what is known. Generate with care; flag for verification.
> 3. **Needs verification before publication:** Claims that require specialist confirmation.
>    These may be used in draft generation; they must be checked before final manuscript.
>
> Do not treat "plausible" as "known-reliable." The verification tier exists precisely
> because the two feel identical when generating prose.

## Known-Reliable: Facts the Novel Builds On

These are historically or scientifically verifiable. The novel must not contradict them.

---

### The Fante Confederation (1868–1873)

**Claim:** The Fante Confederation was formed in 1868 as a political alliance of Fante chiefs
to collectively resist both Ashanti expansion and British encroachment. It drafted a
constitution (the Mankessim Constitution of 1871) and sent formal correspondence to British
colonial authorities in the 1868–1873 period.

**Verification source type:** Published historical scholarship. Key sources: John Mensah
Sarbah's *Fanti Customary Laws* (1897); Adu Boahen, *Ghana: Evolution and Change in the
Nineteenth and Twentieth Centuries* (1975); archival work in the Ghana National Archives
and the Public Record Office, Kew.

**How it appears in the novel:** The Fante Confederation is the institutional frame for
the 1872 diplomatic mission. The novel does not describe the Confederation's constitution
or founding — it is assumed as context. Amara knows it; Kwame researches it; Mara learns it.

---

### The 1874 Anglo-Ashanti War

**Claim:** In 1874, the British Army under Sir Garnet Wolseley defeated Ashanti forces
and sacked Kumasi, burning the palace and extracting a large indemnity. This effectively
ended Ashanti political independence and led to the formal dissolution of the Fante
Confederation's political ambitions, as British Protectorate authority was consolidated.

**Verification source type:** Standard West African colonial history. Well-documented
in British regimental histories, Ghanaian national historical scholarship, and ICOMOS
heritage assessments.

**How it appears in the novel:** Referenced in one sentence in Amara's opening chapter
as the war "everyone knows is coming." This is historically accurate for 1872 — the
political tension was well understood in the region before the formal outbreak of hostilities.

---

### Copper document preservation in saltwater

**Claim:** Copper containers can preserve paper documents for 150+ years in saltwater
if the internal environment approaches near-zero oxygen conditions. This requires the
seal to be effectively airtight, the interior to be free of biological material at sealing,
and the water temperature to be low enough to inhibit bacterial growth.

**Verification source type:** Conservation science. Documented in archaeological literature
on sealed metal containers recovered from underwater sites (e.g., sealed lead capsules at
several Atlantic wreck sites). The Azores thermocline condition is the enabling factor in
this novel: near-constant 11°C at 44m depth inhibits the biological degradation that
would otherwise penetrate a copper seal over time.

**How it appears in the novel:** Mara explains this once, to Alderton, in Chapter 4, in
approximately two sentences. She does not explain it again. It is established as fact and
used.

---

### XRF analysis — non-destructive identification

**Claim:** X-ray fluorescence analysis can identify the elemental composition of materials
(including ink and paper) without destructive sampling. Portable XRF devices (such as
the Olympus Vanta series) are used routinely in field archaeology and conservation science.

**Verification source type:** Conservation science and archaeological field practice.
The Olympus Vanta specifically is a commercially available portable XRF; naming it gives
domain credibility without requiring invention.

**How it appears in the novel:** The XRF result (copper composition consistent with
Liverpool foundry production, 1850–1880) appears in Chapter 4. The analysis takes
approximately 45 seconds per reading. Mara does three readings and averages them.
This level of procedural specificity signals that the scene was written from knowledge,
not from general genre expectation.

---

### Azores thermocline depth and temperature

**Claim:** The Azores thermocline in the mid-Atlantic gyre creates a layer of near-constant
temperature at approximately 40–50 metres depth, around 10–12°C year-round. This is
significantly colder and more thermally stable than surface waters.

**Verification source type:** Physical oceanography. Publicly available oceanographic
data for the Azores archipelago. The specific temperature of 11°C at 44m is a reasonable
figure for the Pico Passage location.

**How it appears in the novel:** Described as Mara's sensory experience in Chapter 2
(the water does not change) and explained technically once in Chapter 4 as the preservation
mechanism for the document. The term "thermocline" appears in the novel; the technical
explanation is brief.

---

### The Fante Confederation's formal correspondence

**Claim:** Fante leaders and their representatives sent formal written correspondence to
British colonial authorities in the period 1868–1873, including to the Colonial Office
in London. These documents exist in the Public Record Office at Kew (CO 96 series, Gold
Coast original correspondence).

**Verification source type:** British National Archives; documented in West African history
scholarship. The specific CO 96 series reference is the publicly available catalogue
designation for Gold Coast colonial correspondence.

**How it appears in the novel:** Referenced by Kwame when he explains what the Ghanaian
archive documents are. The CO 96 series is not named in the novel's prose but is part of
the research foundation that makes Kwame's Accra discovery credible.

---

### Clinker-on-frame ship construction, Liverpool merchant vessels 1860s

**Claim:** Liverpool merchant barques of the 1860s were typically built using clinker-on-
frame construction, with iron frames and wooden planking. A vessel of 420 tons at approximately
55 metres length is consistent with the class of ships used in West African trade in this period.

**Verification source type:** Maritime history; Liverpool shipping records at the Merseyside
Maritime Museum. The ship's name and company are invented; the vessel class is historically consistent.

**How it appears in the novel:** The *Meridian Star*'s physical description (58 metres,
420 tons, clinker-on-frame) appears in the first dive chapter. These are background facts
that establish historical plausibility without requiring the reader to understand ship
construction. Tomás knows the hull type; Mara identifies it from the ROV footage.

## Plausible: Use with Care, Flag for Verification

These claims are consistent with the historical record and domain knowledge but are not
individually documented. They are plausible given what is known. Flag each in the draft
for specialist review before publication.

---

**Claim:** A Fante diplomatic mission seeking to present a case to the Colonial Office in
London would realistically use a Liverpool merchant vessel rather than a formal passage —
given that Fante merchants had established trade relationships with Liverpool firms and that
direct Colonial Office transport was not available to an unofficial delegation.

**Why plausible:** The Fante Confederation's diplomatic activity was explicitly unofficial
from the British perspective (the British did not recognise the Confederation as a sovereign
entity). Using a merchant vessel contracted privately is consistent with this unofficial status.

**Verification need:** A scholar of Fante diplomatic history should confirm whether the
specific routing (Cape Coast → Liverpool via Azores) is consistent with established Atlantic
trade routes for this period.

---

**Claim:** A literate Fante woman with mission school education, working as a steward on
a Liverpool merchant vessel in 1872, keeping an unofficial written log in English, is
plausible given the documented presence of literate Fante mission-school graduates in
colonial commercial contexts.

**Why plausible:** The mission school system in Cape Coast produced literate Fante graduates
from the 1820s onward. The presence of women in supporting roles for merchant and official
travel is documented. The specific combination (woman, literate, steward, log-keeper) is
invented but not implausible.

**Verification need:** A specialist in 19th-century West African women's history should
review whether Amara's background and role are consistent with documented examples from
the period.

---

**Claim:** A Liverpool merchant company operating in West African trade in 1872 would have
routine contact with British colonial administration and sufficient institutional incentive
to suppress documentation of an unofficial diplomatic mission if instructed to do so.

**Why plausible:** The relationship between Liverpool merchant firms and colonial administration
in West Africa is well documented. The specific instruction to falsify a manifest is invented;
the incentive structure is historically consistent.

**Verification need:** A maritime historian with Liverpool trade archive access could
confirm or refute whether instruction documents of this type were recorded in similar
company archives of the period.

## Needs Verification Before Publication

These claims should be used in draft generation — they are not obviously wrong — but must
receive specialist confirmation before the final manuscript is submitted.

---

**Claim:** Interior-applied solder on a copper document cylinder (applied from inside the
cylinder before sealing) would be technically feasible for a person without metalworking
training in 1872, using materials available on a Liverpool merchant vessel.

**Verification needed:** A conservation scientist or metals expert should confirm whether
a lay person in 1872 could achieve an effective copper seal using shipboard materials.
If this is implausible, the novel requires a different mechanism for Amara's sealing
of the cylinder — or the cylinder was prepared professionally before embarkation,
which changes the Chapter 25 scene significantly.

---

**Claim:** The Pico Island harbormaster's log from November 1872 would realistically record
the arrival of an unscheduled merchant vessel by name, with the names of passengers or
notable cargo, in the level of detail Mara requires.

**Verification needed:** A Portuguese maritime archivist or historian with knowledge of
Azorean harbormaster records from the 1870s should confirm what information these logs
typically recorded and whether naming passengers (as opposed to simply logging vessel
name and tonnage) was standard practice.

---

**Claim:** A sealed section of the Torre do Tombo archive covering Azorean maritime
records pre-1900 could plausibly contain Waterhouse & Sons-affiliated documents given
the company's trading routes through the Azores.

**Verification needed:** The Torre do Tombo's actual archival series structure for maritime
records should be checked. "Section C, maritime records pre-1900" is an invented designation.
The plausibility of the inventory and access restrictions described in the novel requires
confirmation from someone with knowledge of the Torre do Tombo's actual restricted collections.

---

## Key Accuracy Policies

1. **Character names:** Amara Quaye (Fante name, correctly gendered). Kwame Osei (Ghanaian,
   male Akan name). Tomás Ferreira (Portuguese, Azorean). These names should not be altered
   without flagging against this document.

2. **Historical dates:** Fante Confederation founded 1868, Mankessim Constitution 1871,
   1874 Anglo-Ashanti War — these are fixed and must not be altered in any scene.

3. **The commission date:** November 1872. This is an invented specific date within the
   historically plausible range of Fante diplomatic activity (1868–1873). Once established
   in Chapter 3, it must be consistent across all chapters.

4. **Ship depth:** 44 metres. Once established in Chapter 1, it must be consistent. The
   thermocline temperature argument depends on the depth being at least 40m.

5. **Copper cylinder interior solder:** This is the preservation mechanism and the evidence
   of Amara's deliberate act. It must be consistent from Chapter 3 (XRF analysis) to Chapter 25
   (sealing scene) to Chapter 26 (photographic documentation).
