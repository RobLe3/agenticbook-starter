# Doc 11: Story Quality — The Glass Meridian

> **Book Lab reference — Lab 11 (Chapter 10):** If you completed Lab 11, compare your story quality framework to this example — look for: craft vs engagement distinction (why a high-scoring chapter can still fail), 3-pillar structure (Plausibility, Human Impact, Momentum).

**3-Pillar Engagement Assessment Framework | Novel-flavor fiction**

---

## Overview

The 16-dimension quality rubric (Doc 08) measures craft. This document measures engagement — the reader's experience of the novel as a story rather than as a piece of prose. A chapter can score well on all 16 craft dimensions and still fail engagement. Engagement failure means the reader does not continue.

The three pillars are: Plausibility, Human Impact, and Momentum. They are assessed separately — a chapter can be strong on one and weak on another. The summary verdict combines all three.

> Note for agent: The distinction between craft quality (Doc 08) and story quality (this document) is not academic. A chapter can be beautifully written but emotionally inert. It can have excellent sentence rhythm but fail to make the reader care about the next chapter. Run both assessments. A chapter needs to pass both.

---

## Pillar 1: Plausibility

**Does the reader believe this world?**

Plausibility is not the same as accuracy. A fantasy novel can be highly plausible. A historical novel can be technically accurate but implausible if the characters do not behave like people. Plausibility is about whether the reader accepts the terms of the fiction and is not thrown out of it by something that feels false.

> Note for agent: This distinction — plausibility vs. accuracy — matters for assessment. When evaluating a chapter, ask "would a skeptical reader be thrown out of the fiction here?" not "is this fact technically correct?" A technically accurate marine archaeology procedure that is described in a way that feels clinical and procedural will fail plausibility even if it passes domain accuracy. The goal is authenticity of texture, not accuracy of specification.

### Assessment Questions

1. Do character decisions follow from their established psychology (Doc 06)? When Mara decides to withhold information from Alderton, does that decision make sense given who we know her to be?
2. Does the 1872 world feel researched, not researched-at? Is the historical knowledge present in the texture of Amara's experience, or in exposition that feels imported?
3. Are the procedural elements — dive archaeology, archival research — depicted with enough accuracy to not break immersion? (Doc 12 governs what is and is not accurate; this pillar governs whether the depiction feels authentic.)
4. When the plot requires something coincidental, is it earned — caused by character choices — rather than convenient?

### What Strong Looks Like

A reader who is skeptical about AI-generated prose reads the marine archaeology scenes and concludes that the author knows what they are talking about. The 1872 chapters feel inhabited — the details are specific and functional, not decorative.

### Failure Mode

Events happen because the plot needs them to. Characters make decisions that are convenient for the story but inexplicable given who they are. Coincidences stack up without being earned. The 1872 world feels like a period drama — all surface atmosphere and no inhabitance.

### Reader Archetype Response

| Archetype | Plausibility Priority | Failure Reaction |
|-----------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Plausibility-Focused (Doc 16, A1) | **Primary pillar** | Puts book down at first false note |
| Human-Impact (A2) | Secondary | Tolerates minor plausibility lapses if emotional truth holds |
| Momentum-Driven (A3) | Low | Will accept implausibility if the pace is maintained |
| Genre-Literate (A4) | High, but sophisticated | Notices convention misuse, not just factual error |

## Pillar 2: Human Impact

**Does the reader care what happens?**

> Note for agent: Human Impact is the hardest pillar for AI-generated fiction because AI defaults to characters-as-argument rather than characters-as-people. The tendency is to use characters to demonstrate the novel's thesis about colonial erasure and historical memory. But a reader who is reading about Mara is not reading to receive an argument — they are reading to be with a person. When Mara makes a mistake, when she is unkind to Kwame because she is frightened, when she cannot finish a sentence about her husband — these are the moments that generate human impact. The historical argument can be present in the texture; it should not be present in the characters' mouths.

### Assessment Questions

1. Is Mara's private grief present even in scenes that are functionally investigative? A chapter in which Mara is entirely absorbed in the work and shows no private dimension has failed this question.
2. Does Amara feel like a full person — with her own desires, her own irritations, her own private assessments of the people around her — or like a historical argument?
3. Does the Alderton betrayal (Chapter 16) land emotionally, not just as information? Has the reader been given enough of Mara's trust in and reliance on Alderton that the revelation costs something?
4. Does the epilogue feel earned or declarative? Has the novel given us enough to believe in this ending, or does it simply announce it?

### What Strong Looks Like

A reader who does not particularly care about colonial history finishes Part II concerned about Mara personally — not just about the historical injustice, but about whether Mara is going to be all right.

### Failure Mode

Characters are carriers of theme. They explain the argument rather than living it. Amara's chapters feel like illustrated historical lectures. Mara's grief is mentioned but not present. The Alderton reveal is treated as a plot event rather than an emotional rupture.

### Reader Archetype Response

| Archetype | Human Impact Priority | Failure Reaction |
|-----------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Plausibility-Focused (A1) | Secondary | Will continue if the world holds, even with thin characterisation |
| Human-Impact (A2) | **Primary pillar** | Will not continue if characters feel like puppets |
| Momentum-Driven (A3) | Low | Does not require emotional depth; requires forward motion |
| Genre-Literate (A4) | Medium | Recognises character-as-thesis as a genre cliché |

---

## Pillar 3: Momentum

**Does the reader keep reading?**

Momentum in literary fiction does not require action. It requires incompleteness — an active question the reader wants answered. The dual-timeline structure creates a specific momentum challenge: each switch between timelines must feel like gaining information, not losing it.

### Assessment Questions

1. Does each chapter end with an unresolved question? Not a cliffhanger — a question. "What is in the cylinder?" is a cliffhanger. "What did Alderton know and when did he know it?" is a question.
2. Is the dual timeline structured so that each switch arrives at a moment when the reader wants to stay AND is rewarded for switching? The switch should feel like both a fulfillment and a new opening.
3. Is Chapter 16 (the midpoint — Alderton's complicity) the genuine centre of gravity, or does the novel drag toward it? Does the narrative build real pressure in Chapters 9–15, or does it tread water?
4. Is the pace tightening in the final quarter (Chapters 22–28)? Are chapters getting shorter, scenes denser, and the dual timelines converging toward a single focus?

### What Strong Looks Like

A reader who started "for 20 minutes" reads for two hours. They do not want to stop at chapter boundaries. When the timeline switches, they feel briefly bereft of the timeline they were in, and then quickly absorbed by the one they have switched to.

### Failure Mode

The historical timeline feels like an interruption to the present investigation rather than a parallel drive. Chapters in either timeline feel padded — scenes that confirm what the reader already knows rather than opening new questions. The final act does not accelerate; it resolves tidily but without urgency.

### Reader Archetype Response

| Archetype | Momentum Priority | Failure Reaction |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------|
| Plausibility-Focused (A1) | Low | Will read slowly; does not require pace |
| Human-Impact (A2) | Medium | Will continue if emotionally invested even without pace |
| Momentum-Driven (A3) | **Primary pillar** | Will abandon if pace fails |
| Genre-Literate (A4) | Medium | Recognises structural padding as a craft failure |

---

## Summary Assessment

After evaluating all three pillars, assign each a signal: Pass / Marginal / Fail.

| Verdict | Criteria |
|---------|---------|
| Proceed | Two or more Pass, none Fail |
| Revise | One Fail OR two Marginal |
| Major Revision | Two or more Fail |

Record the summary assessment in the chapter's production notes alongside the Doc 08 quality score. Both assessments are required before a chapter advances to the verification queue.

## Pillar-to-Archetype Summary Table

| Pillar | A1: Plausibility | A2: Human Impact | A3: Momentum | A4: Genre-Literate |
|--------|-----------------|-----------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Plausibility | Primary | Secondary | Low | High |
| Human Impact | Secondary | Primary | Low | Medium |
| Momentum | Low | Medium | Primary | Medium |

A manuscript that passes all three pillars for A1 and A2 but fails for A3 has a pacing problem, not a quality problem. The revision target is specific: tighten the scenes that tread water and restructure the timeline switches to reward rather than interrupt.
