# Doc 16: Reader Simulation — The Glass Meridian

> **Book Lab reference — Lab 12 (Chapter 12):** If you completed Lab 12, compare your reader simulation to this example — look for: fiction archetypes (inhabitation vs utility framing), 3-of-4 gate threshold, Plausibility-Focused vs Genre-Literate archetype distinctions.

**4 archetypes | Fiction reader simulation | Novel-flavor**

---

## Overview

Reader simulation is the final verification gate in the QVP (Doc 14, Layer 14). It requires running four reader archetypes against the manuscript — each separately, each with its own persona loaded. The gate is: 3 of 4 archetypes return a "continuing" verdict. If 2 or fewer return "continuing," the manuscript requires major revision before moving to production.

The four archetypes for fiction differ fundamentally from the archetypes for non-fiction. Non-fiction archetypes ask: "Can I use this?" Fiction archetypes ask: "Can I live in this?" The underlying question shifts from utility to inhabitation.

> Note for agent: Fiction archetypes are designed around the specific failure modes of literary fiction, not around general reader satisfaction. The Plausibility-Focused reader is not asking "is this accurate?" — they are asking "do I believe this world?" The Genre-Literate reader is not asking "is this good?" — they are asking "have I been here before?" These are different questions, and they require different evaluation protocols.

---

## Archetype 1: The Plausibility-Focused Reader

**Who they are:** A reader who reads widely across literary fiction, historical fiction, and popular history. They will put a book down if a character does something inexplicable or if a historical detail is visibly wrong. They have probably read at least one serious account of the Atlantic world, West African history, or colonial trade routes. They approach fiction with skepticism that they are willing to have overcome.

**What they want from literary fiction:** A world that holds together. They are not reading for action; they are reading to not be thrown out of the fiction. They want to feel that the author has earned the right to make these claims.

**Specific concerns for this novel:**
- Does the 1872 world feel researched — present in the texture of Amara's experience — or researched-at — imported as explanation and period detail?
- Does Mara follow procedures that a real marine archaeologist would follow? Do the dive scenes feel like archaeology or like adventure fiction?
- Does the archive access feel institutionally plausible? Would a reader who has visited a national archive recognize the access dynamics?
- Are the political and diplomatic claims about the Fante Confederation plausible to a reader with some knowledge of the period?

**Evaluation questions to ask this reader:**
- "Would a real marine archaeologist recognise this dive as accurate?"
- "Does the 1872 Fante world feel inhabited, or does it feel like a period drama?"
- "Was there any moment when you stopped believing the world?"

**Continuing verdict sounds like:** "I kept checking things and they held up. The Fante material felt like someone who actually read the period, not just Wikipedia. The dive procedures were right — I know because I checked."

**Flagged verdict sounds like:** "The author clearly read two Wikipedia articles. The archive scene was TV-accurate, not real. The Fante world had no texture — just atmosphere."

---

## Archetype 2: The Human-Impact Reader

**Who they are:** A reader who reads primarily for emotional resonance. They will continue with a difficult or slow novel if they care about the characters. They will put a book down if characters feel like puppets of theme — if the characters exist to make an argument rather than to live.

**What they want from literary fiction:** Mara to feel like a real grieving person who happens to be investigating something important. Amara to feel like a full person — with desires, irritations, and interiority — not a symbol or a historical argument. The emotional stakes to be personal before they are historical.

**Specific concerns for this novel:**
- Is Mara's grief present in her procedural scenes? A chapter in which Mara is entirely absorbed in work, with no private dimension, fails this reader.
- Is the Alderton betrayal (Chapter 16) genuinely devastating, or is it handled as plot information?
- Does Amara have interiority — private observations, private desires — beyond her role as a steward and a narrator of injustice?
- Does the epilogue feel like it was earned by the characters' journeys, or does it feel declared?

**Evaluation questions to ask this reader:**
- "Do you care what happens to Mara personally — not just to the historical record she's uncovering?"
- "Is Amara a person or a thesis?"
- "Did the Alderton reveal land as an emotional event, or just as information?"

**Continuing verdict sounds like:** "I need to know if Mara is going to be okay. Not just whether she solves the case — whether she's going to be okay. That's the book working."

**Flagged verdict sounds like:** "These characters are making arguments. Every scene is designed to say something about colonial history. I understand. I just don't care about any of them."

> Note for agent: The Human-Impact Reader is the hardest archetype to satisfy in AI-generated fiction because AI defaults to characters-as-argument. The tendency is to use characters to demonstrate the novel's thesis. A Mara who exists to uncover injustice is not the same as a Mara who is a grieving woman who happens to find herself uncovering injustice. The difference is visible in small moments: does she notice something irrelevant to the investigation because it reminds her of her husband? Does she make a mistake because she is tired rather than because the plot needs the mistake? These are the moments that satisfy the Human-Impact Reader.

## Archetype 3: The Momentum-Driven Reader

**Who they are:** A reader who reads quickly. They will skip descriptions to get back to the plot. They approach literary fiction with patience but finite patience. They will abandon a literary novel that does not have forward drive, regardless of how good the prose is. They are not hostile to literary fiction; they just need it to move.

**What they want from literary fiction:** Each chapter to end with a question they want answered. The dual timeline to reward rather than interrupt the reading experience. A sense that the investigation is going somewhere, that each chapter has brought them closer to understanding something they did not understand before.

**Specific concerns for this novel:**
- Is the dual timeline structured so that switching feels like gaining speed, not losing it? When the chapter switches from Mara to Amara, does the reader feel they are getting something they needed, or do they feel interrupted?
- Does the investigation move at a pace that doesn't feel padded? Are there chapters that confirm what the reader already knows without adding new questions?
- Is the midpoint (Chapter 16) earning its position as the centre of gravity, or does the novel drag toward it?
- Is the final quarter (Chapters 22–28) compressing — shorter chapters, denser scenes, converging timelines?

**Evaluation questions to ask this reader:**
- "Did you want to skip chapters?"
- "Did each chapter end in a place that made you turn the page?"
- "When the timeline switched, did you feel like you were losing or gaining?"

**Continuing verdict sounds like:** "I told myself one more chapter. And then one more chapter after that. The 1872 sections stopped feeling like interruptions around Chapter 6."

**Flagged verdict sounds like:** "I kept losing the thread when it switched to 1872. And some of those chapters — the Lisbon archive ones especially — I could feel them treading water."

---

## Archetype 4: The Genre-Literate Reader

**Who they are:** A reader who has read enough literary fiction, historical fiction, and mystery to recognise genre conventions and clichés from the inside. They will resist being manipulated by convention. They know what a midpoint reveal looks like; they have read many colonial history narratives; they have encountered the grieving protagonist before. They are not cynical — they want to be surprised — but they will not pretend to be surprised when they are not.

**What they want from literary fiction:** The novel to use genre conventions purposefully — the mystery structure, the dual timeline, the colonial injustice narrative — with enough deliberateness that the conventions feel chosen, not default. They want the Alderton reveal to be surprising but inevitable (the good kind of surprise), not telegraphed. They want the colonial erasure narrative to find the specific emotional weight for this story, not the expected weight for this kind of story.

**Specific concerns for this novel:**
- Is the Alderton reveal handled with sufficient complexity? The best reveals are surprising and yet, in retrospect, obviously coming. The worst reveals are either telegraphed (the reader saw it at Chapter 8) or unmotivated (nothing prepared for it).
- Is the colonial erasure narrative using the right emotional weight? The expected version of this story has a clear moral arc: injustice is exposed, history is corrected. Does this novel find a more specific emotional truth than the expected one?
- Is the dual timeline used purposefully — each timeline doing something the other couldn't do — or is it a convention deployed because this kind of novel uses dual timelines?
- Does the ending feel earned by the specific logic of this story, or does it feel schematic — the ending this kind of novel is supposed to have?

**Evaluation questions to ask this reader:**
- "Did you see every turn coming?"
- "Does the colonial injustice narrative find its specific weight, or does it feel like a familiar argument?"
- "Did the ending feel earned or inevitable in the wrong way?"

**Continuing verdict sounds like:** "It surprised me. The Alderton thing — I should have seen it coming; all the pieces were there — but I didn't. And the ending doesn't give you what you expect. It gives you something harder."

**Flagged verdict sounds like:** "I've read this novel before. Not this one exactly, but this novel. The grieving archaeologist, the colonial cover-up, the old mentor who knew too much. I knew where it was going."

> Note for agent: The Genre-Literate Reader is the hardest archetype to satisfy and the one whose verdict is most instructive when they return "flagged." Their failure mode is predictability — the novel doing what this kind of novel does. When this archetype flags a section, the question to ask is not "what is wrong with the execution?" but "what is the conventional choice that was made here, and is there a less conventional one?" The answer may be in the framing, the sequencing, or the specific emotional register — not necessarily in the plot.

## Simulation Protocol

**Invocation:** Run each archetype separately. Load the archetype description above as the reader persona. Do not run all four archetypes in a single invocation.

**Sample selection:** Use three-chapter samples from:
- Beginning of Act 1: Chapters 1–3
- Midpoint: Chapters 14–16 (the Alderton reveal chapter and its context)
- Final act: Chapters 25–27

**Evaluation:** For each archetype, produce:
1. A "continuing" or "flagged" verdict
2. A specific reason in the archetype's voice (see verdict phrasings above as models)
3. If "flagged": identify which of the three engagement pillars (Doc 11) is failing and in which chapters

**Gate:** 3 of 4 archetypes returning "continuing" = manuscript ready for production.

**If 2 or fewer "continuing":**
- Identify the failing pillar(s) using Doc 11
- Determine whether the failure is manuscript-wide or act-specific
- Target revision at the specific pillar and act
- Run Layer 14 again after revision

## Archetype-to-Pillar Summary

| | Plausibility (Pillar 1) | Human Impact (Pillar 2) | Momentum (Pillar 3) |
|---|---|---|---|
| A1: Plausibility-Focused | Primary | Secondary | Low priority |
| A2: Human-Impact | Secondary | Primary | Medium priority |
| A3: Momentum-Driven | Low priority | Low priority | Primary |
| A4: Genre-Literate | High (sophisticated) | Medium | Medium |

A manuscript that fails A3 but passes A1, A2, and A4 has a pacing problem, not a quality problem. The revision target is specific: tighten the scenes that tread water; restructure timeline switches to reward rather than interrupt. A manuscript that fails A2 across multiple archetypes has a character problem: the characters are carrying theme rather than living it.
