# The Glass Meridian — Premise & Concept

> **Book Lab reference — Labs 1 and 5 (Chapters 1 and 5):** If you completed Lab 1 or Lab 5, compare your premise doc to this example — look for: fiction-specific premise structure vs non-fiction concept doc, dual-timeline setup, scope constraints defined upfront.

**Project:** The Glass Meridian
**Flavor:** Novel (82,000–88,000 words)
**Genre:** Literary mystery / historical thriller
**Structure:** Dual timeline — Timeline A: 2024 (present) · Timeline B: 1872 (past)

---

## Working Title and Subtitle

*The Glass Meridian*

Subtitle (working): *A Novel of Two Timelines*

> **Note for agent:** The title carries two meanings that converge at the novel's climax. A meridian
> is a navigational reference line — a fixed coordinate from which position is measured. "Glass"
> suggests both the fragility of official history (it can be shattered) and the transparency of the
> document that breaks it open. When writing scenes near the resolution, this double meaning should
> become available to the reader — not stated by the narrator, but implied by how Mara handles the
> recovered manifest. Never explain the title in the prose. Let the reader find it.

---

## Logline

A British-Ghanaian marine archaeologist excavating an unregistered 19th-century shipwreck discovers a copper-sealed manifest that proves a Fante diplomatic mission was erased from official history — and must choose between institutional caution and the obligation she feels to the literate steward whose unofficial log made the discovery possible.

> **Note for agent:** The logline is written in present tense regardless of the novel's past-tense
> narration. Present tense in a logline signals "this is happening to someone right now" — it creates
> urgency for the person reading about the project, not the person in the story. A logline in past
> tense reads like a summary of something already finished. The logline also names both protagonists
> implicitly (the archaeologist and the steward) without naming either — this signals the dual-timeline
> structure without requiring explanation.

## Core Premise

**The problem the novel explores:**

The dominant tradition of colonial-history literary fiction reconstructs lost or suppressed African experience through a single narrative strategy: the recovery of oral memory, family story, or community ritual. This is a powerful tradition. It is also, by now, well-mapped territory. Readers who engage with this genre know what they are getting and why it matters.

What is rarer — and what this novel attempts — is a story where the suppression is not complete. The record survived. The document exists. The suppression was not erasure but reclassification: misfiling, institutional neglect dressed as bureaucratic neutrality. This is historically the more common case. It is the harder story to tell: harder because it requires a protagonist who can work within institutions as well as against them, and harder because the antagonist is not malevolence but professional self-interest.

**The story it tells:**

*The Glass Meridian* follows two women separated by 152 years who are doing the same thing: recording what they witness because they are not confident anyone else will. Mara Osei is a marine archaeologist who recovers a copper-sealed document cylinder from a 44-metre wreck off the Azores. The manifest inside references a Fante diplomatic commission to the British Colonial Office in 1872. There is no record of this commission in the Colonial Office files or in any published history of the Fante Confederation. The commission was real. It was suppressed at the point of departure — by a ship's captain who falsified his cargo manifest under instructions from his employer — and the suppression has been maintained, not by active concealment, but by the institutional inertia of archives that do not cross-reference each other.

Amara Quaye is the steward who travelled with the commission in 1872. She kept an unofficial log. Her log survived because she understood, on the last night before a port call that something about, that the only container on the ship that would outlast the commission's failure was a copper-sealed cylinder in the captain's cabin.

> **Note for agent:** The core premise is NOT backstory for characters to recite in dialogue.
> It is the tonal and thematic compass for the novel. When a scene feels flat, return to this:
> is Mara doing the slow, meticulous, bureaucratically obstructed work of recovery? Is the
> obstruction realistic and specific, or is it a generic villain move? When Amara's scenes feel
> like historical colour rather than story, return to this: is she recording because no one else
> will, and does her recording feel like an act with stakes? The premise defines what makes this
> novel different from every other "hidden history" thriller.

**The argument the novel makes about memory and historical erasure:**

Erasure is rarely violent. It is usually procedural. A ship's captain changes a cargo description. An archivist files a collection in the wrong series. A scholar reviews what is authorised rather than what exists. A graduate student follows her supervisor's footnotes without checking what the footnotes omit. *The Glass Meridian* argues that the most durable suppressions are the ones that require no ongoing conspiracy — only the institutional momentum of a mistake that no one has sufficient incentive to correct. The novel's resolution is not a revelation that shocks the establishment. It is a correct filing. A record that was in the wrong place is moved to the right one. That this feels like a victory is the novel's point.

## Market Position: Comparable Titles

- **Aminatta Forna, *The Memory of Love* (2010)** — Parallel timelines, Sierra Leone, institutional memory and suppression of traumatic events. Tone comparator: literary, slow-burn, emotionally precise. *Glass Meridian* fills the gap *Memory of Love* leaves for readers who want the investigative procedural strand more developed and the historical timeline treated as a protagonist's story in its own right, not as contextual backstory.

- **Anthony Doerr, *All the Light We Cannot See* (2014)** — Dual timeline, historical depth, individual-scale story inside massive historical forces. *Glass Meridian* is structurally similar but fills the gap Doerr leaves for readers who want a non-European historical frame and a living investigative process rather than retrospective reconstruction.

- **Esi Edugyan, *Washington Black* (2018)** — Historical, Afro-Atlantic, formally ambitious, concerned with how Black experience is archived and erased inside institutional silences. *Glass Meridian* fills the gap *Washington Black* leaves for readers who want the present-day reckoning dramatised — what it looks like when a researcher actually tries to recover what was suppressed and confronts the institutions still protecting the suppression.

> **Note for agent:** Comparable titles are framed as "fills the gap that X leaves" rather than
> "better than X" or "similar to X." "Fills the gap" is more useful because it defines the
> specific reader need this novel meets — it implies that readers of X exist, that they found X
> valuable, and that this novel offers something X did not provide. This framing is also more
> honest: it acknowledges the quality of the comparable work without claiming equivalence.
> Never write "fans of X will love Y" — this is marketing language that does nothing for the
> agent generating prose. Use the comparable titles to calibrate tone and ambition level.

## Target Reader

Primary: Adults who read literary fiction with investigative or historical themes — readers of Forna, Doerr, Edugyan, and Kate Atkinson who want a novel that respects their intelligence, rewards close reading, and leaves them thinking about institutional power and historical record rather than simply plot resolution. They finish the book and immediately look up the Fante Confederation. They are not bothered by the absence of a car chase.

Secondary: Readers with professional background in archaeology, archival science, maritime history, or West African history who want fiction that gets the domain right. They will notice and appreciate that Mara's decompression schedule is accurate. They will notice if it isn't.

> **Note for agent:** Target reader descriptions are psychological and behavioural, not demographic.
> "Adults 35–55 with university education" tells the agent nothing useful. "They finish the book and
> immediately look up the Fante Confederation" tells the agent exactly what level of confidence to
> write historical detail at — confident enough that the reader wants to verify, not hedged so heavily
> that no detail lands. The secondary reader description ("they will notice if it isn't") tells the
> agent that domain accuracy in this novel is not optional. It is a contractual obligation to a
> portion of the readership.

## Scope Guardrails

> **Note for agent:** These guardrails exist because a dual-timeline novel about colonial history
> and marine archaeology presents enormous scope expansion risk. Every one of these guardrails
> reflects a direction the story could plausibly expand — and must not. When generating a scene
> that starts moving toward any of these, stop and redirect. The guardrails are not limitations
> on ambition; they are the discipline that makes the novel's actual ambition achievable. The most
> common AI failure in scope-rich fiction is defaulting to the genre conventions of adjacent genres:
> thriller (physical threat, chase, explosive climax), romance (romantic subplot with Tomás),
> historical panorama (comprehensive reconstruction of 1872 West Africa). These guardrails name
> those failure modes explicitly so the agent can recognise them when they appear.

1. **This novel does NOT resolve the political question of repatriation.** Mara's discovery raises the question of who owns the recovered documents and what reparative claim the Fante diplomatic mission creates. The novel surfaces this question; it does not answer it. Mara is not a policy advocate. Her work ends when the record is recovered and published. Characters may express views on repatriation. The narrator does not adjudicate between them.

2. **This novel does NOT provide a comprehensive history of the Fante Confederation.** Amara's chapters illuminate the diplomatic mission's specific events in 1872. The broader history of the Confederation (1868–1873) and the 1874 Anglo-Ashanti War is context, not subject. No character delivers more than 2–3 sentences of historical background in a single block of dialogue or narration. If a scene requires more than that, it is doing something wrong.

3. **This novel does NOT follow a thriller pacing model.** There is no chase sequence, no physical threat to Mara, no explosive climax. The tension is institutional and intellectual. Pacing is literary: slow accumulation, a mid-point reversal (Alderton's complicity in Chapter 17), and a resolution that is satisfying but not triumphant. If a scene is generating excitement through physical danger, redirect.

4. **This novel does NOT give Amara a full subplot with secondary characters.** Her world is deliberately compressed — she sees the mission, the ship, the handful of people who share her situation. Expanding her timeline to include developed subplots, additional POV characters, or extended scenes ashore is out of scope. Her chapters are a log, not a panorama.

5. **This novel does NOT explain the marine archaeology process pedagogically.** Mara does her work. The reader observes. Technical terms are defined contextually, not explained. If a scene requires an explanation longer than two sentences, the scene is doing something wrong — Mara already knows this, and the narrator reports her actions, not her internal lectures to herself.

6. **This novel does NOT redeem Dr. Voss.** She is an antagonist with coherent motives, not a villain-who-learns. Her final position is that she was professionally wrong but not personally culpable. The novel accepts this as her honest assessment without endorsing it.

7. **This novel does NOT develop a romantic subplot.** Mara is eighteen months widowed. Tomás is her operational ally. Their relationship deepens through professional trust and mutual recognition, not attraction. Any scene that begins moving toward romantic tension should redirect to the intellectual and practical dynamic between them.
