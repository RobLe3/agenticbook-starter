# Doc 12: Domain Authenticity — The Glass Meridian

> **Book Lab reference — Lab 7 (Chapter 7):** If you completed Lab 7, compare your domain authenticity protocol to this example — look for: "what does NOT happen" sections (preventing TV-accuracy errors), domain-specific fixed procedures, fiction-specific accuracy threshold.

**Marine archaeology + Colonial West African history | Accuracy protocol**

---

## Overview

The Glass Meridian makes claims in two specialist domains: marine archaeology (present timeline) and colonial West African history, specifically the Fante Confederation in the early 1870s (past timeline). Claims in both domains must be accurate enough to withstand scrutiny from a reader with professional knowledge. "Accurate enough" does not mean encyclopedic — it means procedurally and historically plausible, with no errors significant enough to break immersion or undermine the novel's credibility.

> Note for agent: This document serves two functions. The fixed procedures section tells you what must happen — required steps in marine archaeology and required historical context. The "what does NOT happen" sections are equally important: they prevent the most common errors. The most common domain failure mode in AI-generated fiction is depicting procedures as a general audience expects them from television, not as they actually work. Read the "what does NOT happen" sections as carefully as the procedures.

## Part I: Marine Archaeology (Present Timeline)

### Fixed Procedures Mara Must Follow

These steps are not optional or variable. They appear in the novel in some form whenever Mara conducts a dive or removes material from the site.

**Pre-dive: Registration**
Before any material is removed from the *Meridian Star* wreck site, Mara must register the site with the Portuguese Institute of Architectural and Archaeological Heritage (IGESPAR). This registration process is what drives Part I of the present timeline — she knows she has found something significant before she has the authority to touch it. The tension between urgency and procedure is a structural element of the first act.

**Documentation: Survey before contact**
Every dive that involves approaching any artifact includes a photogrammetric survey — a 3D mapping procedure using overlapping photographs — before anything is touched. Every item's location is recorded with a datum point reference. Mara does not pick up the document cylinder before she has mapped it.

**Material retrieval: The cylinder**
The copper document cylinder is retrieved in a sealed container that holds ambient seawater from the site. It is transported to the Marine Research Centre in Horta within four hours of retrieval. The cylinder does not open on the seabed. The cylinder does not open easily anywhere. Processing happens in a lab.

**Decompression**
At 44 meters, every dive requires staged decompression. Mara and Tomás surface in sequence, not simultaneously. They make decompression stops at specified depths on the way up. The dive computer dictates the ascent schedule; they follow it. No dives occur within twelve hours of flying. No more than two dives per day at this depth.

### What Does NOT Happen

> Note for agent: This section exists because the errors it prevents are the most common ones. Each item on this list represents a convention from television archaeology and adventure fiction that is wrong in practice. If you are generating a scene and you are about to write one of these things, stop and consult the fixed procedures above for what actually happens instead.

- **Mara does not pick up artifacts barehanded.** Bare-hand contact with a 150-year-old artifact from a seabed is not done. She wears gloves or uses tools.
- **Mara does not use flash photography underwater at 44 meters.** At depth, artificial lighting rigs (video lights) are used, not flash. Flash at 44m provides poor illumination and is not standard archaeological documentation practice.
- **The copper cylinder does not open easily.** A copper vessel that has been sealed and submerged for 150 years has formed a patina layer that must be carefully addressed in lab conditions. Mara cannot pry it open on the dive boat.
- **Mara does not identify the document on the seabed.** She sees the cylinder, maps it, retrieves it in a sealed container. The contents are unknown until lab processing. The discovery scene is the retrieval, not the identification.
- **Mara does not operate alone.** Every dive involves at minimum Tomás as dive partner. This is both legal requirement and safety protocol.
- **The wreck is not immediately accessible.** Initial dives are reconnaissance. Site registration takes time. The procedural delay is a feature of the plot, not an obstacle to it.

## Part II: Colonial West African History (Past Timeline)

### Fixed Historical Context for 1872

These facts establish the political and cultural landscape in which Amara Quaye's chapters take place. They are historically grounded — not invented for the novel's convenience.

**The Fante Confederation**
The Fante Confederation was politically active from 1868 to 1873. The novel's 1872 events occur in the Confederation's final years — a period of maximum political self-assertion before British formal annexation. The Confederation was attempting to govern its own affairs, manage trade relations, and resist British encroachment. A Fante diplomatic mission to London in this period is historically plausible; the Confederation sent delegations to negotiate directly with the British government on multiple occasions.

**The Gold Coast's political status in 1872**
The British had not yet declared the Gold Coast a formal protectorate in 1872. That declaration came in 1874. The year 1872 sits in a window of contested sovereignty — the Fante had more formal political autonomy than they would have after annexation. This detail matters for the novel's argument: the falsification of the Meridian Star's manifest did not occur under full colonial control. It occurred during a period when a Fante diplomatic commission could plausibly have made a difference.

**Cape Coast**
Cape Coast Castle was the seat of British colonial administration on the Gold Coast in 1872. The Castle is the physical center of colonial power in Amara's world. The Company warehouses (trading company, not military) are the commercial infrastructure. Elder Kofi Mensah's compound represents Fante civil society operating alongside and in tension with both the British and the commercial trading networks.

**Literacy**
Mission school education on the Gold Coast produced literate Fante men and, in smaller numbers, women, by the 1860s. Amara's literacy — she was educated at the Cape Coast missionary school — is historically plausible. She is not exceptional in being literate; she is somewhat unusual in being employed in a position that allows her literacy to matter.

**The falsification**
Cargo manifest falsification was a criminal offense under 1872 Board of Trade regulations. Captain Calder's falsification of the manifest under orders from Waterhouse & Sons places him in legal jeopardy — which is part of why the suppression extended to the log and the diplomatic commission records. The falsification is not merely dishonest; it is criminal, and the parties involved knew it.

### What Does NOT Happen in 1872 Chapters

- **Amara does not think in twenty-first century framing.** She does not observe colonial power structures with contemporary analytical distance. She observes them from inside them, with the political consciousness available to a literate Fante person in 1872 — which is sophisticated, but differently so.
- **Anachronistic language.** No terms, concepts, or social formations that did not exist in 1872 enter Amara's narration or dialogue. When in doubt about period accuracy, err toward the generic rather than toward the anachronistic.
- **Calder does not have modern sensitivities about what he is doing.** He is not a villain who knows he is a villain. He is a ship's master following commercial orders, operating within a system of colonial commerce that he regards as legitimate. His dialogue and behavior reflect this.

## Part III: Challenge Protocol

When new research or a reader's correction contradicts a domain claim in the manuscript:

1. **Identify the specific claim** in the manuscript text.
2. **Locate the claim in the CFR** (Doc 13). Determine its HC entry number.
3. **Assess the tier** using Doc 10 (Canon Rules). Is this a Tier 1, 2, or 3 fact?
4. **For Tier 3:** Adjust the manuscript text locally. Note the correction and its source in the VFR (Doc 15) under the relevant V-entry.
5. **For Tier 2:** Flag the conflict. Propose a fix that preserves Tier 1 facts. Update the CFR and VFR entries. Await human approval before applying the fix.
6. **For Tier 1:** Flag to human author immediately. Do not propose a fix. Do not proceed with further chapter generation until the Tier 1 conflict is resolved.
7. **Document the correction** in the VFR (Doc 15): date, specific claim, source of correction, resolution.

> Note for agent: The challenge protocol exists because historical scholarship develops and regulations change. A marine archaeology procedure that was accurate in February 2026 may have been updated by a regulatory change. A historical claim about the Fante Confederation may be challenged by new archival work. The protocol prevents improvised corrections that solve one problem and create three others downstream. When in doubt: flag and stop rather than fix and continue.
