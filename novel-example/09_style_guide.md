# Doc 09: Style Guide — The Glass Meridian

**Novel-flavor | Literary mystery / historical thriller | Dual timeline**

## Overview

This document is the authoritative prose standard for The Glass Meridian. Every chapter draft is checked against these rules before scoring. When a scoring dimension and a style rule conflict, the style rule wins — the scoring rubric measures how well the style is executed, not whether to follow it.

---

## Part I: Voice

### Register

The prose register is literary — precise, unhurried, emotionally attentive. This is not academic prose (no argument being made through the narration) and not thriller prose (no propulsive pace-above-all). The quality the prose is aiming for is careful thought observing itself: the sense that the narration is paying close attention to what it notices, and that the act of noticing is itself significant.

> Note for agent: "Precise, unhurried, emotionally attentive" is a compass, not a formula. When in doubt, prefer the specific detail over the general statement, the slower sentence over the fast one, and the underwritten emotional moment over the explained one.

### Mara's Chapters

Mara's prose has a formal surface over emotional depth. She observes with precision; her grief surfaces in what she does not say. When Mara is in professional mode — running a dive, examining a document, writing a report — the prose is tightly controlled. When the control slips, it does so briefly and returns. The reader should feel the effort of the control, not just the control itself.

Her sentences tend toward the declarative. She does not editorialize. She records.

**Signal phrases to watch for:** Any sentence in which Mara explicitly names her own emotional state ("she felt sad," "she was overwhelmed") is a flag. Her emotional state should appear in what she notices, what she skips, what she cannot bring herself to finish.

### Amara's Chapters

Amara's prose is sharper, more ironic, less formal than Mara's. She observes her employers with the detachment of someone performing invisibility — someone who has learned to be present and unremarked simultaneously. Her language operates in two registers:

- **Internal voice:** Twi-inflected cadence. Sentences can move differently — compound structures, rhythms that do not match Victorian English. This is not dialect; it is rhythm and perspective.
- **Performed steward voice:** Formally correct English. When Amara speaks aloud in the 1872 timeline, she speaks as expected of her role. The gap between the internal and performed voice is where the novel's political argument lives.

**Signal phrases to watch for:** Any moment in which Amara's internal narration sounds like polished Victorian prose is a flag. Her interiority should feel like someone who has learned to translate themselves, not someone who inhabits that translation.

> Note for agent: The two POV voices are the most important craft differentiator in this novel. If you cover the chapter heading and cannot tell from the first paragraph whether you are in Mara's chapter or Amara's, the voice differentiation has failed. This is the most common failure mode in AI-generated dual-timeline fiction — the prose defaults to a unified register and the chapter heading does all the work.

## Part II: Point of View

**Rule:** Third person limited, deep. No head-hopping within scenes. One POV character per chapter, always.

**Free indirect discourse (FID):** The primary technique for conveying character interiority. The character's thought-world enters the narration without "she thought" tags. The reader inhabits the character's perspective through the narration itself, not through reported thought.

**Example of what to avoid:**
> She thought that the document cylinder must have been sealed specifically to protect its contents.

**Example of FID:**
> The cylinder had been sealed specifically. Someone had known what was inside and had wanted it to last.

The second version does not signal whose thought this is — the reader fills in that it is Mara's inference. This is FID working correctly.

**Irony rule:** The reader may know something the POV character does not — but only through what the POV character notices without understanding. The narration does not wink at the reader. It does not step outside the POV character's knowledge to signal the irony. The reader earns the irony by paying attention to what the character fails to understand.

> Note for agent: POV discipline is stated this explicitly because POV leakage is the most common AI failure in literary fiction. The narration will slip into brief omniscience — a sentence that reports what another character is feeling, or a narratorial observation that the POV character could not have made. Run a POV audit on every chapter: every sentence should be something the POV character could see, think, infer, or notice.

## Part III: Tense

- **Present timeline (Mara's chapters):** Past tense.
- **Past timeline (Amara's chapters):** Past tense.

Both timelines are written in past tense. The "distance" of 1872 is established through vocabulary, cultural context, and the specifics of Amara's worldview — not through tense shift.

> Note for agent: The most common beginner error in dual-timeline fiction is using present tense for the "present" chapters to signal contemporaneity. This novel does not do that. Past tense for both timelines is a deliberate choice: it puts both Mara's investigation and Amara's voyage at the same narrative distance, which is part of the novel's argument about how we hold historical time.

## Part IV: Structural Conventions

### Chapter Openings

Begin in scene. No "It was a Tuesday" generalities. The first paragraph establishes physical location and the POV character's attention within three sentences. The reader should know within the first paragraph: where we are, whose eyes we are seeing through, and what that person is attending to.

### Chapter Endings

End on a shift. The shift can be:
- A new piece of information that changes what the reader understands
- A decision by the POV character (not announced, but made)
- An image that resonates with the chapter's thematic material

**Do not end on:** Action-thriller cliffhangers. The chapter ending is not a hook — it is a completion that opens a question. The question should be intellectual or emotional, not suspenseful.

### Section Breaks

Three asterisks (`***`) centered on their own line. Maximum two per chapter.

### Dialogue

| Character | Dialogue Convention |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Mara | Spare. She listens more than she speaks. When she speaks, it is precise. She does not make small talk. |
| Amara (performed) | Formally correct English. The register of someone who has been trained to perform competence in a foreign language. |
| Amara (internal) | Does not appear as dialogue — appears as prose narration in her internal voice. |
| Tomás | Direct, practical. Short sentences. He is not a philosopher. |
| Helena Voss | Oblique. Never says what she means directly. Her meaning lives in the gap between what she says and what she does not say. |
| Calder (1872) | Blunt, managerial. Commands, not requests. |
| Kwame | Faster than Mara. More comfortable with ambiguity. His journalist's habit of questioning shows in how he structures statements as questions. |
| Alderton | Donnish, paternalistic, indirect — which is how he has maintained the suppression for 20 years without ever quite lying. |

## Part V: Jargon Policy

### Marine Archaeology Terms

Use accurate terminology without explanation when Mara uses the terms in her own professional context. The reader is with Mara; they will absorb the context. When introducing technical terms to a non-specialist character (Kwame, a journalist, or a scene with Portuguese port authority officials), add a small contextual gloss — not a definition, but enough surrounding language that the meaning is inferable.

**What to avoid:** The parenthetical definition. Not "photogrammetric survey (a 3D mapping technique using overlapping photographs)." The technique should be mentioned in context of its use.

### 1872 Colonial Terms

Use period-accurate terms in Amara's chapters. Add small contextual signals to orient contemporary readers — not modern explanations, but enough texture that the reader can infer the meaning from context and power dynamics.

**What to avoid:** Anachronistic language. A glossary of terms potentially offensive by contemporary standards is not required — what is required is that no character uses language that did not exist in 1872. Amara does not think in twenty-first century framing. She thinks in 1872 framing, which is a different thing from thinking in 1872 clichés.

> Note for agent: Period accuracy in Amara's chapters is both a craft and an ethical responsibility. The novel is making an argument about how colonial history was recorded and suppressed. Amara's voice should embody that history from the inside, not observe it from a safe contemporary distance. When in doubt: specific period detail over general historical atmosphere.

## Part VI: Structural Element Policy

This is literary fiction. The structural conventions differ from non-fiction.

- **No callout boxes within chapters.** The manuscript has no tcolorbox environments, no sidebars, no margin notes within chapters.
- **Epistolary elements:** Amara's log excerpts, when they appear within chapters, are set as block quotations. In the LaTeX manuscript, these will receive slightly different typography (handled in the preamble, not in this doc). In the source text, set them off with a blank line above and below and with a markdown blockquote marker.
- **Chapter headers:** Chapter number + timeline marker in brackets. Format: `Chapter 7 [PRESENT]` or `Chapter 8 [PAST]`. The timeline marker appears on its own line below the chapter number.
- **No chapter epigraphs** unless explicitly approved per chapter in Doc 05 (narrative architecture). Epigraphs are not a default structural element.

## Part VII: What Good Looks Like

A chapter that executes this style guide well has the following qualities:

1. You can cover the chapter heading and identify the POV character from the first paragraph.
2. The most emotionally significant moment in the chapter is underwritten — the reader feels it without being told to.
3. Every marine archaeology or colonial history term that appears is either contextually clear or appropriately glossed for a non-specialist character present in the scene.
4. The chapter ends with a question — not a cliffhanger, but something incomplete that the reader wants to resolve.
5. No explicit thought-attribution markers survive: "she thought," "she felt," "she wondered if" are converted to FID or cut.
