# Target Readers — Sourdough at Home

**Project:** Sourdough at Home: A Practical Guide to Wild Yeast Baking
**Flavor:** Topic (55,000–65,000 words)

> **Note for agent:** Topic-flavor requires 4 reader archetypes (vs. Short-flavor's 2).
> When writing any chapter, consider whether content serves all four or primarily one.
> Chapters that serve only one archetype should be brief and clearly scoped.

---

## Persona 1: The New Baker

**Who they are:** Adults who have baked from a box mix but never made bread from scratch.
They are curious about sourdough because they have seen it on social media or tasted an
exceptional loaf at a friend's house. They are not yet sure if this is a passing interest
or a serious hobby.

**What they need:** A clear starting point with no assumptions. They need to know what
equipment to buy, how to create a starter from nothing, and how to manage the process
without it taking over their life.

**What they fear:** Wasting money on flour that produces nothing edible. Keeping a
"pet" starter alive indefinitely (they have heard stories). The process feeling too
complicated to fit into a busy schedule.

**Reading behavior:** Reads front to back. Follows instructions sequentially. May not
read chapter headers — assumes linear reading is the correct path.

**Jargon tolerance:** Very low. Any technical term (hydration, bulk ferment, autolyse)
must be defined on first use and used consistently thereafter. No assumed knowledge of
bread baking vocabulary.

**Success signal:** Produces a loaf that rises, has an open crumb, and tastes distinctly
sour. Does not need to be perfect — just recognizably sourdough.

---

## Persona 2: The Transitioning Baker

**Who they are:** Experienced home bakers who have been baking yeast breads for years.
They make reliable sandwich loaves, pizza dough, and enriched breads using commercial
yeast. They are switching to sourdough because they want more flavor complexity, a
longer shelf life, or better digestibility.

**What they need:** A framework for translating their existing bread knowledge into
sourdough practice. They do not need an explanation of gluten development from scratch —
they need to understand how wild yeast behaves differently from commercial yeast and
why their existing timing instincts will mislead them.

**What they fear:** Wasted effort on a process that produces inferior results to their
current practice. Being told to unlearn everything when most of their knowledge transfers.

**Reading behavior:** Reads selectively. May skip introductory chapters and jump to
the starter chapter, then to shaping and baking. Uses the index. Notices inconsistencies
because they have an existing mental model to compare against.

**Jargon tolerance:** High for standard baking vocabulary (gluten, hydration, windowpane
test). Low for fermentation science vocabulary (Lactobacillus, acetic vs. lactic acid
balance). Bridge vocabulary between baking and fermentation science is valuable.

**Success signal:** Produces a sourdough loaf with better flavor complexity than their
commercial yeast equivalent. Understands why the process differs and can adapt timing
to their schedule.

## Persona 3: The Troubleshooter

**Who they are:** Bakers who have attempted sourdough before and failed at some point.
They may have a starter that "never worked," loaves that never rose, or bread that is
either too dense or too sour. They have read at least one other sourdough book or
watched multiple YouTube videos without resolving their specific problem.

**What they need:** Diagnosis before prescription. They do not want to be told to "start
over" — they want to understand what went wrong and how to fix it without discarding
their existing work. They need a troubleshooting vocabulary that maps their observed
outcome to a specific process failure.

**What they fear:** Receiving the same generic advice (keep your starter warmer, use
better flour) without explanation. Being asked to retry the same process that already failed.

**Reading behavior:** Non-linear. Searches for their specific failure mode. Reads
troubleshooting sections first, then works backward to understand the underlying cause.
May have a starter already and jump directly to diagnosing it.

**Jargon tolerance:** Medium. Has encountered terminology but may have misunderstood it.
Explicit correction of common misconceptions is valuable (e.g., "a starter that passes
the float test is not necessarily ready to use").

**Success signal:** Identifies the specific cause of their previous failure, corrects it,
and produces a loaf that is measurably better than their previous attempts.

## Persona 4: The Fermentation Curious

**Who they are:** People whose primary interest is in wild fermentation broadly —
gut health, probiotics, traditional food preservation, or the microbial science of
fermented foods. They may also brew kombucha, make kimchi, or culture yogurt. Sourdough
is one application of a broader interest.

**What they need:** The science. They want to understand the microbial ecology of a
sourdough starter, the chemistry of fermentation byproducts, and why the process produces
health-relevant compounds. They are reading a practical baking guide but will be most
engaged when the science appears.

**What they fear:** Being given instructions without explanation. Reading yet another
book that says "the wild yeast makes the bread rise" without explaining the yeast strain
diversity, the role of bacteria, and the pH dynamics involved.

**Reading behavior:** Reads the science notes carefully. May skip "common beginner
mistakes" sections. Particularly attentive to the "Why This Works" boxes. May reference
the book's technical claims against external sources.

**Jargon tolerance:** High for science vocabulary. Appreciates precision. Will notice
oversimplifications. Can tolerate Lactobacillus sanfranciscensis, pH 3.5–4.5, and
acetic vs. lactic acid ratio discussions.

**Success signal:** Understands the biological mechanisms behind sourdough fermentation
well enough to explain them to someone else. Produces consistent results because they
understand the variables rather than following steps blindly.

## Jargon Policy Summary

| Term | First Use Location | Definition Required |
|------|--------------------|---------------------|
| Hydration | Ch 1 §Equipment | Yes — weight-based ratio definition |
| Bulk fermentation | Ch 3 §Process | Yes — time × temperature explanation |
| Autolyse | Ch 4 §Technique | Yes — before vs. after starter addition |
| Windowpane test | Ch 4 §Gluten | Yes — what you see, what it means |
| Proofing / proof | Ch 5 §Shaping | Yes — distinguish bulk proof from final proof |
| Oven spring | Ch 6 §Baking | Yes — steam + starch gelatinization |
| Crumb | General | Define open, irregular, and tight/dense |
| Starter / levain | Ch 1 §Starting | Yes — levain as sub-type, not synonym |
| Discard | Ch 1 §Feeding | Yes — not waste; unbaked starter removed |
