## Chapter Map

### Introduction: Why Sourdough Is Hard (and Why That's Good News)

**Intent:** Frame the problem (inconsistency without process understanding), introduce
the book's core approach (process-science bridge + integrated troubleshooting), and give
readers a taste of the diagnostic vocabulary they will develop.

**Primary reader:** All four archetypes. Introduction must earn each one.

**Word target:** 2,500 words

**Lab:** None (orientation only)

---

### Chapter 1: Equipment and Starting Materials

**Intent:** Establish the minimal viable equipment set. Resist the urge to list every
available tool. Name the three items that cannot be substituted (kitchen scale, Dutch
oven, instant-read thermometer) and explain why each is non-negotiable.

**Primary reader:** New Baker (setting up for the first time), Transitioning Baker
(may have some equipment but needs to confirm gaps)

**Word target:** 4,000 words

**Lab 1:** Equipment inventory — verify you have the three non-negotiable items and
identify substitution options for each secondary tool.

**Key claims (track in Doc 07):**
- Kitchen scale accuracy: ±1 gram
- Minimum Dutch oven capacity: 4 quarts (Dutch oven heat retention spec)
- Thermometer response time: ±2 seconds ideal

---

### Chapter 2: Building and Maintaining a Starter

**Intent:** Create a working starter from flour and water. Explain the microbial
succession that occurs during the first 7–10 days. Give readers a diagnostic framework
for evaluating starter health rather than relying on sensory guesswork.

**Primary reader:** New Baker + Fermentation Curious (science content is highest here)

**Word target:** 5,000 words

**Lab 2:** Starter creation — begin your own starter and document daily observations
using the starter log template (Appendix A).

**Key claims (track in Doc 07):**
- Starter creation duration: 7–14 days for reliable activity
- Optimal starter temp: 75–78°F (24–26°C)
- Feeding ratio: 1:5:5 (starter:flour:water) by weight
- Float test caveat: float test is not a reliable readiness indicator

---

### Chapter 3: The Fermentation Process — What Is Actually Happening

**Intent:** Explain the biology of fermentation at a level useful for diagnosis:
yeast produces CO₂ (lift), Lactobacillus produces acids (flavor and preservation),
temperature controls the ratio of acetic to lactic acid production.

**Primary reader:** Fermentation Curious (primary) + Troubleshooter (needs this to diagnose)

**Word target:** 4,500 words

**Lab 3:** Controlled fermentation observation — run a bulk ferment at two different
temperatures and document the difference in rise volume and timing.

**Key claims (track in Doc 07):**
- Sourdough pH range: 3.5–4.5
- Cool ferment (<70°F): more acetic acid (sharper flavor)
- Warm ferment (>78°F): more lactic acid (milder, yogurt-like)
- Overfermentation threshold: >80°F accelerates bacterial activity beyond yeast

---

### Chapter 4: Mixing and Gluten Development

**Intent:** Explain the autolyse technique, introduce the windowpane test, and
establish why sourdough dough handling differs from commercial yeast dough
(slower fermentation means more time for enzymatic activity during mixing).

**Primary reader:** Transitioning Baker (most likely to bring commercial yeast habits)

**Word target:** 4,000 words

**Lab 4:** Windowpane test practice — mix dough with and without autolyse; compare
gluten development at the same time point.

**Key claims (track in Doc 07):**
- Autolyse duration: 20–60 minutes (30 min is standard for beginners)
- Bread flour protein: 12.0–13.5%
- Whole wheat protein: 13.0–14.5%
- Optimal mixing temp for dough: 75–77°F (24–25°C)

---

### Chapter 5: Bulk Fermentation

**Intent:** Define bulk fermentation, establish the rise-volume method (50–75% increase)
as the primary readiness indicator, explain how temperature affects timing, and
introduce the stretch-and-fold technique.

**Primary reader:** All four archetypes. This is the highest-failure chapter.

**Word target:** 5,000 words

**Lab 5:** Timed bulk ferment — record start volume, temperature, and end time across
at least two bakes. Compare against the 50–75% target.

**Key claims (track in Doc 07):**
- Bulk ferment readiness: 50–75% volume increase (not reliant on float test)
- Bulk ferment duration at 75°F: 4–6 hours
- Stretch-and-fold frequency: every 30–45 minutes for first 2 hours
- Overfermentation sign: dough pulls apart when stretched (lost structure)

---

### Chapter 6: Shaping

**Intent:** Teach three reliable shapes (round boule, oval batard, open fougasse).
Explain pre-shape vs. final shape, the bench rest purpose, and how to develop surface
tension without deflating the dough.

**Primary reader:** New Baker + Transitioning Baker

**Word target:** 4,000 words

**Lab 6:** Shape and compare — produce one boule and one batard from the same batch of
dough. Compare oven spring and crumb structure.

**Key claims (track in Doc 07):**
- Bench rest duration: 15–30 minutes (longer in warm kitchen)
- Cold retard (final proof in refrigerator): 8–16 hours at 38–40°F (3–4°C)

---

### Chapter 7: Baking

**Intent:** Explain the Dutch oven method, the purpose of steam in the first 20 minutes,
the relationship between initial steam (oven spring) and final crust development (lid removal),
and how to score for controlled expansion.

**Primary reader:** New Baker (highest stakes chapter — baking is irreversible)

**Word target:** 4,000 words

**Lab 7:** Temperature log — record Dutch oven surface temperature before adding dough,
oven temperature at 20-minute mark, and finished internal bread temperature.

**Key claims (track in Doc 07):**
- Preheat Dutch oven duration: 45–60 minutes at target temp
- Baking temperature: 475–500°F (246–260°C)
- Steam phase (lid on): 20 minutes
- Crust development phase (lid off): 20–25 minutes
- Finished internal temperature: 205–210°F (96–99°C)

---

### Chapter 8: Temperature Control

**Intent:** Make temperature a first-class variable throughout the process. Provide
time-temperature curves for kitchens from 65°F to 80°F. Introduce the Desired Dough
Temperature (DDT) calculation for controlling water temperature.

**Primary reader:** Troubleshooter + Transitioning Baker

**Word target:** 4,500 words

**Lab 8:** Kitchen temperature mapping — measure ambient temperature at 6 points in your
kitchen and identify the optimal fermentation location.

**Key claims (track in Doc 07):**
- DDT formula: (DDT × 3) − (flour temp + room temp + friction factor)
- Friction factor for hand mixing: ~0°F; for stand mixer: ~25°F

---

### Chapter 9: Flour Types and Substitutions

**Intent:** Explain the role of protein content, bran content, and ash content in
sourdough. Give readers a practical framework for flour substitution without sacrificing
structure. Cover bread flour, all-purpose, whole wheat, and rye.

**Primary reader:** Transitioning Baker + Fermentation Curious

**Word target:** 4,000 words

**Lab 9:** Flour comparison — produce the same recipe with bread flour vs. all-purpose.
Record rise volume and crumb comparison.

**Key claims (track in Doc 07):**
- All-purpose flour protein: 10.0–12.0% (variable by brand)
- Rye flour ash content: higher than wheat; accelerates fermentation
- Rye % recommendation for starter: 5–20% of total flour

---

### Chapter 10: Hydration and Dough Feel

**Intent:** Demystify hydration as a concept. Explain why high-hydration doughs (>80%)
are difficult for beginners and how to work up gradually. Connect hydration to crumb
structure (open crumb requires both high hydration AND proper fermentation).

**Primary reader:** New Baker (who has seen open-crumb photos and wants to replicate them)

**Word target:** 4,000 words

**Lab 10:** Hydration ladder — produce three loaves at 70%, 75%, and 80% hydration.
Document handling difficulty and crumb differences.

**Key claims (track in Doc 07):**
- Beginner hydration recommendation: 70–75%
- Open crumb hydration minimum: 75–80% (but requires proper fermentation to achieve)
- Wet hands method: add small amounts of water to counter slippage

---

### Chapter 11: Scheduling and Life Integration

**Intent:** Address the practical challenge that sourdough is time-sensitive and life
is not. Explain the refrigerator retard technique (cold bulk fermentation), the
overnight proof, and how to pause the process without destroying progress.

**Primary reader:** New Baker + Transitioning Baker (schedule-management questions)

**Word target:** 3,500 words

**Lab 11:** Schedule design — create a weekly baking schedule that fits your specific
weekly calendar.

**Key claims (track in Doc 07):**
- Cold bulk ferment: 8–14 hours at 38–40°F slows but does not stop fermentation
- Same-day bake: possible with 8-hour total timeline in warm kitchen

---

### Chapter 12: Discard and Zero-Waste Baking

**Intent:** Address the discard question directly (new bakers feel guilty discarding
starter). Explain what discard is, why it is necessary, and how it can be used for
pancakes, crackers, and pizza dough.

**Primary reader:** New Baker (environmental concern) + Transitioning Baker (wants practical applications)

**Word target:** 3,500 words

**Lab 12:** Discard recipe — produce one discard recipe using this week's discard.

---

### Chapter 13: Troubleshooting — A Diagnostic Framework

**Intent:** Provide a systematic framework for diagnosing sourdough failures. Organized
by symptom (flat loaf, dense crumb, too sour, not sour enough, scored crust tears wrong,
spread instead of rise) → cause → fix.

**Primary reader:** Troubleshooter (this is their chapter)

**Word target:** 5,000 words

**Lab 13:** Problem log — document your three most common failure symptoms and trace
each to a process cause using the diagnostic framework.

---

### Chapter 14: Conclusion — What Comes Next

**Intent:** Affirm the reader's progress. Point to natural next steps without scope
creep (whole grain baking, flavored inclusions, non-bread applications). Frame
consistency as the destination, not perfection.

**Primary reader:** All archetypes

**Word target:** 2,000 words

**Lab:** None
