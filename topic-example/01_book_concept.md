# Book Concept — Sourdough at Home

**Project:** Sourdough at Home: A Practical Guide to Wild Yeast Baking
**Flavor:** Topic (55,000–65,000 words)
**Genre:** Non-fiction how-to / practical guide

---

## Problem Statement

Most sourdough books fall into two failure modes. The first type gives readers beautiful
photographs and aspirational recipes but no working explanation of what is actually
happening inside the dough. When something goes wrong — a starter that never activates,
a flat loaf, dense crumb, uncontrolled oven spring — these readers have no framework for
diagnosis. They try random fixes and eventually conclude they lack "the touch."

The second type goes deep into fermentation biochemistry, microbial ecology, and grain
science. This precision is admirable, but the audience for a 400-page technical treatise
on Lactobacillus sanfranciscensis is narrow. Most home bakers want practical guidance
that actually works, not a literature review.

The result is a gap: skilled practitioners who can reliably produce excellent sourdough
cannot explain their success in a way that transfers to readers, and scientifically
rigorous authors cannot translate their knowledge into a practical framework beginners
can follow in a kitchen with one oven, one Dutch oven, and unreliable room temperature.

**The specific failure this book solves:** Bakers who attempt sourdough without
understanding the relationship between time, temperature, and fermentation activity
will produce inconsistent results indefinitely — not from lack of skill, but from
lack of a diagnostic framework.

---

## Unique Value

**1. The process-science bridge.** Every technique in this book is paired with a brief
explanation of why it works at the level of wild yeast and bacteria. This is not
a science lecture — it is a diagnostic vocabulary. When your bulk ferment takes 7 hours
instead of 4, you will know why and you will know how to adjust. No other beginner-to-
intermediate sourdough guide provides this pairing consistently across all techniques.

**2. A troubleshooting framework built in, not bolted on.** Most sourdough books include
a troubleshooting appendix. This book integrates troubleshooting as a running diagnostic
thread: each chapter ends with a "What went wrong?" section that directly maps outcomes
to causes using the process vocabulary taught in that chapter. By the end of the book,
readers have a complete diagnostic system, not a disconnected Q&A list.

**3. Temperature as a first-class variable.** Sourdough results are dominated by
temperature — of the kitchen, the water, the dough. Most books note temperature in
passing. This book makes temperature tracking a core practice from Chapter 1 and provides
calibrated guidance for kitchens from 65°F to 80°F. Recipes include time-temperature
curves, not single time estimates.

---

## Scope Guardrails (What This Book Does Not Cover)

1. **Commercial yeast baking.** This book is exclusively about wild yeast fermentation.
   Commercial yeast is mentioned only in the introductory chapter to orient readers who
   are transitioning from it.

2. **Pastries, cakes, or enriched doughs.** The book covers lean breads (sourdough
   discard recipes are a brief appendix). Croissants, brioche, and other enriched doughs
   are a different skill set and are not covered.

3. **Milling and grain sourcing.** This book uses commercially available whole wheat,
   bread flour, and rye flour. Home milling and single-origin grain sourcing are beyond
   scope — not because they are unimportant, but because they introduce variables that
   would require a separate book.

4. **Commercial-scale production.** This is a home baker's guide. Equipment, volumes,
   and processes are sized for a single domestic oven with one or two Dutch ovens.
   Commercial scheduling, proofing chambers, and deck ovens are not covered.

5. **Advanced shaping techniques.** The book teaches three reliable shapes (round boule,
   oval batard, and open-faced fougasse). Baguettes, specialty shapes, and scoring
   patterns beyond a basic cross-score are not included in the main text.

---

## Market Position

- **Level:** Beginner to intermediate (someone who has heard of sourdough and wants to
  actually do it, or someone who has tried and failed and wants to understand why)
- **Closest comparables:** *Tartine Bread* (Chad Robertson) — beautiful, aspirational,
  but technique-heavy and photo-led. *The Sourdough School* (Vanessa Kimbell) — health
  and nutrition focus. *Modernist Bread* — far too technical for this audience.
- **Differentiator:** None of these provide the process-science bridge + integrated
  troubleshooting that this book prioritizes.
