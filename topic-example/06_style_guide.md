# Style Guide — Sourdough at Home

> **Book Lab reference — Lab 6 (Chapter 6):** If you completed Lab 6, compare your style guide to this example — look for: voice specificity, jargon policy precision, structure rules. Note: Short-flavor does not have a style guide; this is a Topic-flavor addition.

**Project:** Sourdough at Home: A Practical Guide to Wild Yeast Baking
**Flavor:** Topic (55,000–65,000 words)

> **Note for agent:** Read this document before every writing session.
> It defines the rules that make 14 chapters sound like one author.
> Violations accumulate; catching them in revision is costly.

---

## Part I: Voice and Tone

### Primary Voice: Practical Authority

The book is written by a practitioner who has baked hundreds of loaves, watched hundreds
of students fail for the same reasons, and developed a diagnostic vocabulary that actually
helps. The voice is:
- **Authoritative but not academic.** This is expertise you can act on, not expertise
  you admire from a distance.
- **Patient but not condescending.** Readers are capable adults who happen to not know
  this yet. Explain clearly; do not lecture.
- **Precise but accessible.** Temperature ranges are exact. Science explanations use
  accurate terms. But the complexity stays at the level of "useful to a home baker,"
  not "useful for a microbiology thesis."

### What to Avoid

- **Aspirational language without substance.** Phrases like "your kitchen will fill
  with the warm aroma of..." are acceptable in the introduction and conclusion.
  They are not acceptable in process chapters. Process chapters earn trust through
  precision, not atmosphere.
- **Over-hedging.** "Some bakers believe..." "It is often said that..." — do not use
  these when a direct statement is correct. "Most sourdough failures are caused by
  temperature" is preferable to "many bakers report that temperature may be a factor."
- **Cheerful vagueness.** "Just keep practicing and you'll get it!" — not acceptable.
  Identify the specific practice that improves the specific outcome.

### Appropriate Humor

Rare, dry, and directed at universal baking experiences (not at the reader's presumed
incompetence). One example per chapter maximum. A paragraph about the author's own
early failures (concrete, specific) is acceptable in the introduction.

## Part II: Jargon Policy

### Define on First Use

Every term listed in Doc 04 (Key Concepts) must be defined the first time it appears
in the manuscript. Format: introduce the term in bold, then define it in the same
sentence or the next sentence.

Example:
> "During **bulk fermentation** — the first fermentation stage, before shaping —
> the dough expands as yeast produces carbon dioxide."

### Canonical Term Usage

Use the canonical term from Doc 04 exactly as written. Do not:
- Substitute synonyms ("culture" for "starter")
- Use abbreviations not defined in Doc 04 ("BF" for "bulk fermentation")
- Use colloquial alternatives that appear in other books ("mother" for "starter")

Exception: after a term is fully defined and used multiple times, it may be shortened
if the shortened form is unambiguous in context. "The bulk ferment" is acceptable
after "bulk fermentation" is established.

### Science Note Jargon

Technical terms (Lactobacillus, Saccharomyces cerevisiae, acetic acid) are permitted
in Science Note callout boxes. In main text, use accessible equivalents:
- "lactic acid bacteria" not "Lactobacillus"
- "wild yeast" not "Saccharomyces cerevisiae"
- "sharp, vinegary acid" not "acetic acid" (unless already introduced in a Science Note)

## Part III: Structure Rules

### Chapter Opening Format

Every chapter opens with a single-paragraph hook (2–4 sentences) that places the reader
in a specific baking moment. The hook should present a problem the reader recognizes.

Example acceptable hook:
> "The timer went off two hours ago. Your dough has barely moved. You are standing in
> your kitchen trying to decide whether to press on or start over. This chapter gives
> you the tools to make that decision with confidence rather than guessing."

Do NOT open with a quote from another author. Do NOT open with a definition.

### Section Headings

All chapter sections use **sentence case** (capitalize only the first word and proper
nouns). Do not use title case for section headings.

Correct: "Why temperature matters more than time"
Incorrect: "Why Temperature Matters More Than Time"

### Numbers and Units

- Temperatures: always provide both Fahrenheit and Celsius in parentheses on first use
  per chapter. Subsequent uses may use Fahrenheit only.
  - Format: "75°F (24°C)" for first use; "75°F" for subsequent use.
- Weights: grams are primary. Ounce equivalents in parentheses for US readers.
  - Format: "500g (17.6 oz)" for first use; "500g" for subsequent use.
- Percentages: always use baker's percentages (weight-based, relative to flour weight).
  - Exception: "75% hydration" and similar ratios are clear from context.
- Time: hours and minutes. No decimals. "4 hours 30 minutes" not "4.5 hours."

### Process Steps

When describing a process with discrete steps, always use an ordered list (not a
paragraph that describes steps sequentially). Maximum 8 steps per list. If more than 8,
split into phases with a heading.

### Callout Box Policy

Use the following callout box types only:

| Box Type | When to Use |
|----------|-------------|
| **Tip** | A practical shortcut or variation that helps but isn't required for success |
| **Warning** | A failure mode the reader should actively avoid; a step where errors are irreversible |
| **Science Note** | A brief explanation of the underlying biology or chemistry that makes a technique work |
| **Troubleshooting** | A "What went wrong?" section — every core process chapter ends with one |

Do NOT use generic "Note:" boxes. Assign every callout to one of the four types above.
Maximum 2 callout boxes per chapter section. Maximum 4 per chapter.

## Part IV: Numeric Precision Policy

### Acceptable Ranges

All technical claims use ranges derived from the Volatile Facts Register (Doc 09):
- Temperatures: ±2°F acceptable variation noted (e.g., "75–78°F")
- Hydration: ±2% acceptable variation (e.g., "70–75%")
- Times: ±30 minutes for fermentation times; ±5 minutes for baking times

### Do Not Narrow Beyond Canon

If Doc 09 specifies "bulk ferment at 75°F: 4–6 hours," the manuscript must say
"4–6 hours" — not "5 hours" (artificially precise) or "3–8 hours" (over-widened).
All narrowing must be justified in Doc 07 (Canon Rules) before appearing in text.

---

## Part V: Repetition and Redundancy Policy

### Define Once, Reference Consistently

Technical terms are defined once in their chapter. Subsequent references use the term
without re-definition unless the chapter is > 60 pages from the definition.

### Cross-References

Use "See Chapter N" references sparingly — maximum 2 per chapter. Prefer brief
in-text reminders ("as established in Chapter 5, bulk fermentation readiness is
indicated by a 50–75% volume increase") over bare cross-references.
