## Category 1: Starters and Cultures

### Starter (also: sourdough starter, wild starter)

**Definition:** A living fermentation culture made from flour and water, containing
wild yeast (primarily Saccharomyces cerevisiae) and lactic acid bacteria (LAB,
primarily Lactobacillus species). The starter is maintained by regular feedings of
fresh flour and water. It leavens bread by producing carbon dioxide and contributes
flavor by producing organic acids.

**Confusion risk:** "Starter" and "levain" are sometimes used interchangeably.
In this book, "starter" is the maintained mother culture; "levain" is a portion
of starter refreshed specifically for a single bake.

---

### Levain

**Definition:** A preferment made from a portion of starter, fresh flour, and water,
refreshed 4–12 hours before baking. Using a levain (rather than adding starter directly)
allows the baker to control the fermentation profile for a specific bake without
affecting the long-term maintenance culture.

**Confusion risk:** Many bakers use "starter" and "levain" interchangeably. This book
uses them as distinct terms: the starter is the ongoing culture; the levain is the
baking-day preparation.

---

### Discard

**Definition:** The portion of starter removed during regular feedings to control total
volume. Discard is not waste — it is active starter at a lower activity level than
a recently fed starter. It is suitable for use in discard recipes (crackers, pancakes,
pizza dough) where leavening is supplemented by baking powder or commercial yeast.

---

### Feeding Ratio (also: hydration ratio for starters)

**Definition:** The weight ratio of starter to fresh flour to fresh water added during
each feeding. A 1:5:5 ratio means 1 part starter + 5 parts flour + 5 parts water.
Higher ratios (1:10:10) dilute the starter more, extending time to peak activity.

**Canonical value:** 1:5:5 (starter:flour:water) for standard home maintenance.

---

## Category 2: Flour and Ingredients

### Protein Content

**Definition:** The percentage of protein by weight in a flour, primarily gluten-
forming proteins (glutenin and gliadin). Higher protein content produces stronger
gluten networks, which can hold more gas and produce more structure in the final loaf.
Relevant range for sourdough: 10–14%.

**Canonical values:**
- All-purpose flour: 10.0–12.0%
- Bread flour: 12.0–13.5%
- Whole wheat flour: 13.0–14.5%
- Rye flour: 8.0–13.0% (lower gluten-forming proteins; accelerates fermentation via amylase)

---

### Hydration

**Definition:** The ratio of water to flour by weight, expressed as a percentage.
A dough with 75% hydration contains 75g of water for every 100g of flour.
Higher hydration produces a more open crumb structure but is harder to handle.

**Canonical values:**
- Beginner recommendation: 70–75%
- Intermediate target: 75–80%
- Advanced open-crumb: 80–90% (requires strong fermentation control)

---

### Autolyse

**Definition:** A rest period after initial mixing of flour and water (before adding
starter and salt) that allows the flour to fully hydrate and gluten to begin
organizing without mechanical mixing. Typically 20–60 minutes.

**Why it matters:** Autolyse reduces total mixing time and improves dough extensibility.
It also allows the baker to assess flour absorption before adding starter.

**Confusion risk:** Some recipes call for adding starter during autolyse ("modified
autolyse"). This book uses the traditional autolyse (water + flour only).

---

### Pre-fermented Flour Percentage (also: poolish, biga — not used in this book)

**Definition:** The percentage of total flour that is fermented before the final mix.
In sourdough, this refers to the flour in the levain. Typical range: 10–20%.
Higher percentages increase flavor complexity and reduce final proof time.

---

## Category 3: Process

### Bulk Fermentation (also: bulk proof, first fermentation)

**Definition:** The first fermentation stage, during which the mixed dough ferments
as a single mass (before shaping). During bulk fermentation, yeast produces CO₂
that expands the dough, and bacteria produce organic acids that develop flavor.
Readiness is indicated by a 50–75% volume increase.

**Canonical values:** Duration at 75°F (24°C): 4–6 hours.

**Confusion risk:** "Proof" is sometimes used for both bulk fermentation and final proof.
This book uses "bulk fermentation" for the pre-shape stage and "proof" or "final proof"
for the post-shape stage.

---

### Stretch and Fold

**Definition:** A periodic folding technique performed during bulk fermentation to
develop gluten strength without deflating the dough. The dough is gently stretched
upward, then folded over itself. Performed every 30–45 minutes during the first
2 hours of bulk fermentation.

---

### Pre-shape

**Definition:** A gentle preliminary shaping performed after dividing the dough (if
making multiple loaves) and before the bench rest. Pre-shape creates a rough round
that allows the gluten to relax before final shaping.

---

### Bench Rest

**Definition:** A 15–30 minute uncovered rest after pre-shape, during which gluten
relaxes and the dough becomes easier to shape. Duration depends on kitchen temperature
(warmer kitchen → shorter bench rest).

**Canonical value:** 15–30 minutes.

---

### Final Proof (also: proofing, second rise)

**Definition:** The fermentation stage after final shaping, during which the shaped
dough is placed in a banneton (proofing basket) or bowl lined with a floured cloth.
The final proof may be conducted at room temperature (2–4 hours) or in the refrigerator
(8–16 hours cold retard).

---

### Cold Retard (also: cold proof, retarding)

**Definition:** Slowing fermentation by placing the shaped dough in the refrigerator
for an extended final proof (8–16 hours at 38–40°F / 3–4°C). Benefits: improved
flavor complexity, flexible scheduling, and easier scoring (cold dough holds its shape).

**Canonical value:** 8–16 hours at 38–40°F (3–4°C).

---

## Category 4: Baking

### Oven Spring

**Definition:** The rapid final expansion of the loaf during the first 10–15 minutes
of baking, driven by rapid CO₂ expansion (from yeast) and water vapor expansion
(from dough moisture). Oven spring is maximized by: sufficient fermentation, proper
scoring, and steam in the initial bake phase.

---

### Steam Phase

**Definition:** The initial phase of baking with the Dutch oven lid on (or using
another steam trapping method). Steam prevents the crust from setting prematurely,
allowing maximum oven spring. Duration: 20 minutes.

**Canonical value:** 20 minutes lid-on.

---

### Crust Development Phase

**Definition:** The second phase of baking after the lid is removed. Without steam,
the crust sets and browns via Maillard reaction. Duration: 20–25 minutes.

---

### Desired Dough Temperature (DDT)

**Definition:** The target temperature of mixed dough before bulk fermentation begins.
Controlling DDT requires adjusting water temperature to compensate for flour temperature
and ambient temperature. DDT affects bulk fermentation rate and flavor development.

**Formula:** Water temp = (DDT × 3) − (flour temp + room temp + friction factor)
where friction factor ≈ 0 for hand mixing, ≈ 25°F for stand mixer.

---

## Category 5: Troubleshooting

### Float Test

**Definition:** A test for starter readiness in which a small amount of starter is
dropped into water. A starter that floats is actively producing gas and is a candidate
for use. However, the float test is not a reliable sole indicator of readiness.

**Confusion risk:** Overreliance on the float test is a common beginner error. A starter
may pass the float test before its microbial activity is optimal for baking. This book
recommends using the float test as one data point alongside visual (dome shape, bubbles)
and timing cues.

---

### Overfermentation

**Definition:** Bulk fermentation carried beyond the 50–75% rise volume target.
Signs include: dough that pulls apart when stretched (lost structure), very strong
sour smell, and a dough that spreads rather than holding shape. Overfermented dough
produces dense crumb and a flat loaf.

**Temperature trigger:** Kitchens above 80°F significantly accelerate fermentation.

---

### Underfermentation

**Definition:** Bulk fermentation ended before the 50% rise volume target is reached.
Signs include: dense dough that feels tight, very low oven spring, closed crumb.

---

### Dense Crumb

**Definition:** A finished loaf with small, irregular air pockets rather than an open,
airy interior. Primary causes: underfermentation, underproofing, or insufficient gluten
development. Secondary cause: high hydration without sufficient fermentation time.

---

### Gummy Crumb

**Definition:** A finished loaf with a damp, sticky interior that feels undercooked
despite reaching correct internal temperature. Primary cause: insufficient bake time
or cutting the loaf before it has fully cooled (internal steam redistribution requires
1–2 hours of cooling time).

---

## Category 6: Science Notes

### pH in Fermentation

**Definition:** A measure of acidity (potential of hydrogen). Pure water is pH 7 (neutral).
Sourdough reaches pH 3.5–4.5 during fermentation due to organic acid production by
Lactobacillus species. Lower pH (more acidic) inhibits spoilage bacteria and improves
shelf life.

**Canonical value:** Sourdough pH 3.5–4.5 at peak fermentation.

---

### Lactic vs. Acetic Acid

**Definition:** The two primary organic acids produced by Lactobacillus in sourdough.
Lactic acid produces a mild, yogurt-like sourness and forms preferentially in warm,
wet environments. Acetic acid produces a sharp, vinegary sourness and forms preferentially
in cool, dry environments (lower hydration, lower temperature).

**Practical implication:** Control flavor profile by adjusting temperature and hydration.

---

### Saccharomyces cerevisiae (wild strain)

**Definition:** The dominant yeast species in most sourdough starters. Wild S. cerevisiae
is more acid-tolerant than commercial baker's yeast, which allows it to survive
alongside Lactobacillus bacteria. It is also more sensitive to temperature than
commercial yeast.
